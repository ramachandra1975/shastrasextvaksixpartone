The Grammar the Body Forgot

Vaikharī, Sphoṭa, and Dhvani: Recovering the Semiotics of Gesture for Contemporary Practice

A study of the documented commentarial argument that codified gesture (abhinaya) extends vaikharī — spoken language's own externalizing act — together with an honest, feature-by-feature test of how far that extension holds, why the understanding has eroded in contemporary practice, and what recovering it enables.

July 2026

A Note on Method

This paper draws on a documented commentarial tradition — Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya, the Nāṭyaśāstra, Abhinavagupta's Abhinavabhāratī, and Ānandavardhana's Dhvanyāloka — rather than on general cultural impression. Each claim below is tested against a stated evidentiary standard before it is accepted: a claim is treated as established only where a named technical term, a locatable textual passage, and an articulable argument from that passage's own premises can be produced together. Where a claim fails this standard, or succeeds only partially, that is reported plainly. This discipline is what separates the argument that follows from the slogans this paper argues against.

Executive Summary

Classical Indian linguistic philosophy developed one of the most rigorous accounts anywhere of how meaning moves from an inner, unspoken grasp to a form another person can perceive. Bhartṛhari's grammar names the terminal stage of that movement vaikharī: full externalization into a form perceptible to another. Centuries later, a specific and well-documented commentarial argument — found in Abhinavagupta's Abhinavabhāratī — extends this category to codified physical gesture (abhinaya), treating it as a further instance of the same externalizing act rather than a separate, merely decorative accompaniment to speech.

This white paper reconstructs that argument to its own evidentiary standard and tests it, feature by feature, against the tradition's strongest further claims: that gesture manifests meaning the way sphoṭa theory says sound does; that a fixed gestural vocabulary combines as productively as a phoneme inventory does; that gesture can generate full sentence-level meaning independent of speech; and that it therefore qualifies as fully linguistic. The honest finding is not a flat yes or no. Gesture is licensed as vaikharī's further reach with respect to one specific property. It is conventionally coded, not naturally manifestational, in the sphoṭa sense. It shares a phoneme inventory's shape but not its combinatorial productivity. It operates in coordination with speech, not independently of it. And it is best modeled by dhvani — suggested meaning evoked for a qualified perceiver — rather than by the stronger claims often popularly attributed to it.

This precision has largely been lost, replaced by two opposite failures: romantic overclaiming ("every gesture is a word") and dismissive underclaiming (gesture as mere decoration). Both have costs — for pedagogy, for how "expressiveness" is judged, for gesture-recognition technology, and for cultural policy. This paper argues for a third position: gesture is a real, rigorously describable, partially-linguistic system, and understanding exactly what it can and cannot do is more valuable than either exaggerating or dismissing it.

Part I — What the Tradition Actually Claims

1.1 Vaikharī and the Extension to Gesture

Bhartṛhari's Vākyapadīya describes speech unfolding through four stages, from an undifferentiated intuition (paśyantī) through an internally rehearsed but still unspoken form (madhyamā) to vaikharī — audible, externalized speech, complete and perceptible to another. Vaikharī's defining property, on this account, is not its acoustic substrate as such but its function: it is the stage at which an internally grasped meaning becomes available to someone else.

Centuries later, Abhinavagupta's Abhinavabhāratī — his commentary on the Nāṭyaśāstra's treatment of dramatic gesture — makes a specific, locatable, and technically named argument: that codified gesture (abhinaya) is an aupacārika prayoga, a figurative but substantive extension of vaikharī's own category. The argument is not from resemblance and not from shared aesthetic effect. It is from vaikharī's own defining property: gesture, like audible speech, externalizes an internally grasped meaning into a form another can perceive — differing only in which sense-channel receives it.

A functional-equivalence argument on one defining property licenses extension with respect to that property specifically — not automatically with respect to every further property vaikharī happens to have.the discipline this paper follows throughout

This is a real, evidenced philosophical claim, not folklore. It is also, deliberately, a narrow one: it licenses treating gesture as vaikharī's further reach for one specific reason, and nothing more, until each further property is separately tested.

1.2 Sphoṭa: What It Requires, and Why Gesture Doesn't Simply Inherit It

Sphoṭa theory is Bhartṛhari's account of how a sequence of transient sounds manifests a single, unified meaning-bearing unit. It carries three load-bearing features: (a) the manifesting cause is a temporally sequenced, discretely segmented string of sound-events; (b) the manifested unit is grasped as a single, unanalyzed whole, not assembled piece by piece; and (c) the relationship between sound and meaning is one of manifestation — the sound reveals a real universal — rather than arbitrary convention alone.

Tested independently against codified gesture, each feature fares differently. Feature (a) holds only partially: a held hand-configuration has no internal temporal sequence at all, and even a moving gestural phrase unfolds continuously rather than in the discrete, digitally segmented steps a phoneme string requires. Feature (b) is plausibly satisfied — trained observers appear to grasp a gesture's meaning holistically rather than parsing it piece by piece — but this rests on pedagogical description and structural analogy rather than direct experimental confirmation.

Feature (c) is decisive, and it fails for the general case. The classical hasta-inventory is documented as taught name by name, meaning by meaning — the pattern one would expect of a conventional (saṅketa-governed) sign-system, not one where the shape itself discloses its meaning. A small, bounded minority of directly iconic gestures partially escapes this — much as onomatopoeia is a bounded exception within an otherwise arbitrary spoken vocabulary — but a marked exception does not overturn a documented general rule.

1.3 The Hasta/Varṇa Parallel: Real, but Partial

The Sanskrit phoneme inventory (varṇamālā) and the classical hand-gesture inventory (hasta) share genuine structural features: both are finite, both are individually named and taught as a closed curriculum, and both are categorized digitally rather than on a continuous gradient.

The parallel breaks on the feature that gives spoken language its expressive reach: productivity. Roughly fifty phonemes generate, through rule-governed combination, the entirety of the Sanskrit lexicon. The hasta inventory does not do this. Individual gestures typically carry their meaning directly, and narrative sequences are documented as concatenative — independently meaningful units stitched into a scene by the audience's own narrative inference — rather than generative, capable of producing meanings never previously listed or heard.

Benchmarked against other digital sign-systems, the hasta inventory's profile resembles heraldry or a fixed signal system far more than a numeral system or a phonological grammar, both of which achieve real productivity from a small digital base.

1.4 Coordination, Not Independence

The Nāṭyaśāstra defines abhinaya, from the point of first definition, as constitutively fourfold: āṅgika (bodily), vācika (verbal), āhārya (costume), and sāttvika (involuntary psychophysical states) — not a later elaboration, but built into the term's own definition, with all four channels ordinarily deployed together.

This coordinated-not-independent reading survives its hardest documented test. Solo, gesture-forward traditions relocate the verbal channel — a vocalist sings the narrative while the performer's hands and face carry gesture — rather than eliminating it. And in the rare segments where gesture approaches pure nṛtya with no verbal channel at all, meaning narrows toward mood and affect rather than sustaining sentence-level narrative content.

Crucially, this is not a claim that gesture in general cannot be full grammar. Natural sign languages are established in the linguistics literature as full grammatical systems — spatial verb agreement, classifier constructions, non-manual grammatical markers — engaging the same left-perisylvian language networks as spoken language. The deciding factor for linguistic status is documented grammatical structure, not channel, and codified dramatic gesture has not been shown to possess that structure.

1.5 Linguistic Status: Conventional, Not Fully Generative

The Nāṭyaśāstra's own lokadharmī / nāṭyadharmī distinction — ordinary worldly behavior versus stylized dramatic convention — bears directly here. A codified sorrow-gesture does not resemble sorrow the way an unstylized expression does; its meaning must be learned. This satisfies a genuine necessary condition for linguistic status: the sign is conventionally, not naturally, paired with its meaning. Necessary is not sufficient, however — the sphoṭa-style manifestation relationship and generative productivity are both independently tested above and both found wanting. Gesture clears one bar and not the others.

1.6 The Better Model: Dhvani Over Sphoṭa

If sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship and generative productivity don't describe how gesture carries meaning, a different model is needed. Dhvani theory, developed by Ānandavardhana, names a suggested sense (vyaṅgya) an expression (vyañjaka) evokes without stating it directly. Unlike sphoṭa, dhvani does not require a discretely segmented manifesting cause — a held expression suggests as well as an unfolding one. It is built to accommodate context-dependent polysemy as a central case, matching the documented phenomenon of one hasta carrying several senses resolved by context. And it explicitly requires a qualified perceiver (sahṛdaya), matching the tradition's own emphasis on trained spectatorship.

This paper treats dhvani-over-sphoṭa as the tradition's own best synthetic proposal — clearly flagged as a scholarly proposal built on convergent evidence, not a classical equivalence the texts state outright.

Part II — Where the Understanding Has Been Lost

2.1 From Rigorous Argument to Inherited Label

The documented reception history of the vaikharī-extension argument shows exactly how precision erodes. Abhinavagupta's original argument engages specific, checkable conditions. Later, more widely circulated practical manuals inherit the label without re-arguing, or even restating, its grounds. The rigorous version survives mostly in theoretical commentary a working performer or general audience rarely encounters.

2.2 Symptom One: Gesture Taught as Vocabulary Without Grammar

A pedagogy that teaches "this hasta means this" without teaching register, combinatorial limits, or the coordination principle is teaching vocabulary without grammar — the gestural equivalent of memorizing word-meaning pairs without ever learning how words combine into sentences.

2.3 Symptom Two: Conflating "Expressive" With "Linguistic"

Slogans like "dance is a universal language" assert a strong, technical claim on the evidence for a weaker, aesthetic one. The cost runs both ways: it invites easy debunking, and that debunking then gets mistakenly applied to the real, more modest claim the evidence does support.

2.4 Symptom Three: Loss of the Coordinated Model in Practice

Some contemporary staging presents gesture as free-standing "storytelling without words," implicitly claiming the coordinated system's full narrative content survives when its coordinating verbal channel is removed. The evidence suggests otherwise: what an audience receives in that condition narrows toward mood and affect.

2.5 The Cost of the Lost Precision

  • Cultural: transmission drifts from a living, reasoned system toward a fixed aesthetic surface.
  • Scientific: a rich set of testable distinctions goes largely untested in cognitive science — an underused source of hypotheses.
  • Technological: gesture-recognition design frequently re-derives, without acknowledgment, distinctions this tradition worked out centuries earlier.

Part III — Why This Matters Now: Modern Parallels and Stakes

3.1 Sign Language Linguistics: Proof the Channel Was Never the Limit

William Stokoe's demonstration that American Sign Language is a full grammatical system — developed further by Bellugi, Klima, Sandler, and Lillo-Martin — is the single most important external check available. Natural sign languages possess exactly the features codified dramatic gesture lacks: productive combinatorial morphology, formal grammatical marking, and the capacity for genuinely novel propositions. The question is never channel; it is documented structure.

3.2 Embodied Cognition and Multisensory Integration

Decades of audiovisual speech-perception research — the McGurk effect and its follow-up literature — demonstrate that spoken and gestural channels are ordinarily processed as one bound communicative event. This independently confirms the coordination principle the fourfold abhinaya scheme argues for.

3.3 Gesture Studies: An Independently Convergent Framework

David McNeill's research on co-speech gesture — developed independently of the Sanskrit material — concludes that gesture and speech form a single, integrated system. Two entirely independent traditions of inquiry converging on a coordinated model is a data point worth taking seriously.

3.4 Human-Computer Interaction and Gesture-Recognition Design

Practical gesture-recognition systems must decide whether a vocabulary is discrete (classifiable) or continuous (estimable along a gradient) — precisely the digital-versus-analog distinction this tradition drew centuries ago, and one it applied unevenly and carefully rather than uniformly.

3.5 Movement and Dance Therapy

Clinical and therapeutic movement literature increasingly calls for precise, describable vocabularies of gesture-meaning rather than impressionistic language about "authentic expression" — exactly the toolkit this framework already offers.

Part IV — Applications: What Rigorous Understanding Enables

For Performing Arts Pedagogy

Training should teach the coordination principle and register distinctions alongside vocabulary, equipping performers to explain their art form with precision rather than slogans.

For Cognitive and Linguistic Science

The tradition's own distinctions are already well-specified, testable hypotheses — an invitation for direct experimental work, particularly on holistic-versus-componential gesture perception.

For Technology Design

Gesture-recognition systems should distinguish discrete/digital vocabularies from continuous/analog channels at the design stage, informed by this same distinction.

For Cultural Policy

Preservation frameworks should recognize these traditions as rigorous semiotic systems worthy of serious scholarly study, not only as heritage or folklore categories.

Part V — A Framework for Recovery

Guiding Principles

  • Precision over slogan: state what the evidence actually supports.
  • Register-awareness: distinguish naturalistic from stylized representation.
  • Coordination-awareness: be honest about what is lost when one channel is removed.
  • Qualified-perceiver awareness: train audiences, not only performers.
  • Testability: treat the tradition's distinctions as hypotheses, not settled doctrine.

Recommendations by Stakeholder

  • Performers and teachers: incorporate register and coordination principles explicitly into training.
  • Scholars and scientists: design direct empirical tests of the tradition's own stated distinctions.
  • Technologists: consult the digital/analog and productivity distinctions in interaction design.
  • Cultural institutions and policymakers: fund and frame these traditions as intellectually substantive systems.

Limitations and Counterarguments

The dhvani-over-sphoṭa conclusion is this paper's own considered synthesis of convergent evidence, not a classical equivalence the source texts state outright — it should be taught as a well-evidenced proposal, not settled doctrine.

The parallels drawn to sign-language linguistics, embodied cognition, and gesture studies are genuine, but readers should not mistake structural resemblance for proof that every classical claim is scientifically validated. Sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship, for instance, is a metaphysical commitment not straightforwardly testable by neuroscience at all.

This paper centers on one textual line — the Nāṭyaśāstra through Abhinavagupta to Ānandavardhana — and living regional traditions vary considerably; a finding here should not be assumed to hold identically everywhere without separate examination.

Finally, some readers may reasonably feel a linguistically precise analysis undersells the aesthetic or devotional dimensions of these art forms. This paper's aim is to add rigor to how gesture's communicative structure is described, not to reduce these traditions to only what passes a linguistics examination.

Conclusion

The claim that codified gesture extends spoken language's own externalizing function is real, documented, and evidenced — and it is also, honestly examined, narrower and more precise than either its enthusiasts or its skeptics usually credit. Gesture is not simply speech in another channel, and it is not mere decoration either. It is a conventionally coded, coordinated, dhvani-style suggestive system — structurally comparable in shape to a phonemic inventory but not in combinatorial power — best understood on its own precisely stated terms rather than through borrowed slogans. Recovering that precision is the difference between transmitting a living, reasoned system to the next generation and transmitting only its surface.

Appendix — Glossary of Key Terms

Vaikharī
The final stage in Bhartṛhari's account of speech: fully externalized, audible language, perceptible to another person.
Sphoṭa
The single, unified meaning-bearing unit that a sequence of sounds is held to manifest.
Dhvani
Suggested sense (vyaṅgya) evoked by an expression (vyañjaka) beyond direct denotation; the central concept of Ānandavardhana's poetics.
Abhinaya
Dramatic expression in the fourfold sense: bodily (āṅgika), verbal (vācika), costume-based (āhārya), and psychophysical (sāttvika).
Hasta / Mudrā
A codified hand-configuration in classical Indian dance and drama, drawn from a fixed, named inventory.
Aupacārika Prayoga
Figurative or extended application of an established category to a new case — the mechanism by which gesture extends vaikharī.
Lokadharmī / Nāṭyadharmī
Representation modeled on ordinary worldly behavior versus stylized dramatic convention.
Nṛtta / Nṛtya
Pure, non-representational rhythmic movement versus expressive, meaning-bearing movement.
Sahṛdaya
The "same-hearted" qualified perceiver dhvani theory requires.
Saṅketa
Convention: an agreed, learned pairing between a sign and its meaning, as opposed to a natural relationship.

Chapter 1 · RQ1 Cluster

Restating the Problem With Full Precision

Original argument: Sets the evidentiary bar (named term + locatable passage + stated-premise argument) before any claim about gesture is made.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

वैखर्या हि कृतो नादः परश्रवणगोचरः । मध्यमया कृतो नादः स्फोटव्यञ्जक उच्यते ॥

vaikharyā hi kṛto nādaḥ paraśravaṇagocaraḥ | madhyamayā kṛto nādaḥ sphoṭavyañjaka ucyate ||

Source: Widely cited in the Vākyapadīya tradition (vicinity of Vākyapadīya 1.144, on the vaikharī/madhyamā relation to sphoṭa) — verify exact verse number against your working edition (Rau or Iyer) before print.

Translation: The sound produced by vaikharī is what falls within the range of another's hearing; the sound produced by madhyamā is said to be the revealer (vyañjaka) of the sphoṭa.

Prakriyā (how it works here): This is the verse the whole RQ1 cluster stands on. It states plainly that vaikharī's defining job is to be perceptible to another (paraśravaṇagocaraḥ) — externalization-for-a-receiver. Abhinavagupta's aupacārika prayoga argument (Ch. 2–3) extends exactly this property, and only this property, to codified gesture: gesture is also, functionally, something made perceptible to another. The verse also quietly does the work of Ch. 4's boundary condition — it names vaikharī's job as making sound audible, not as revealing the sphoṭa directly (that's madhyamā's job); so a property shared at the vaikharī level doesn't automatically inherit sphoṭa's manifestational status. That separation is exactly what Ch. 9–16 goes on to test.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: a textually anchored basis for saying gesture is licensed as vaikharī's further reach for one specific reason — perceptibility-for-another — and no more than that reason, until each further property is tested on its own.

Acoustic

Sets the evidentiary bar the way a signal-detection experiment pre-registers its criterion before collecting data — decide what counts as signal before you look, so noise can't be rationalized into a finding.

Neuroscience

Parallels the discipline of not inferring a shared neural mechanism from surface behavioral similarity alone — two tasks feeling alike doesn't mean shared cortical circuitry.

Analogous to defining, before assay, what counts as a true synthesis pathway versus a coincidental byproduct — precision up front prevents post-hoc storytelling.

Chapter 9 · RQ2 Cluster

Restating Sphoṭa's Three Load-Bearing Features

Original argument: (a) sequenced manifesting cause, (b) unitary grasping, (c) manifestation vs. convention — restated independently before testing.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

chatur vāṇī catvāri vāk parimitā padāni

catvāri vāk parimitā padāni tāni vidur brāhmaṇā ye manīṣiṇaḥ | guhā trīṇi nihitā neṅgayanti turīyaṃ vāco manuṣyā vadanti ||

Source: Ṛgveda 1.164.45 (the celebrated catvāri-vāk mantra, later read by the grammarian tradition as the scriptural seed of the four-stage speech doctrine).

Translation: Speech is measured in four steps; the wise who have insight know them. Three, hidden in the cave, do not move; men speak the fourth [step] of speech.

Prakriyā (how it works here): This mantra is the scriptural anchor later grammarians (including commentators in Bhartṛhari's lineage) point to for a graded, multi-stage model of speech — three stages hidden (paśyantī, madhyamā, and a third often read as parā) and a fourth spoken openly (vaikharī). It matters for RQ2 because sphoṭa's three features (Ch. 9) are properties of how the hidden stages relate to the spoken one — the manifesting cause, the unitary grasp, the manifestation-versus-convention question all concern that inner/outer relationship. Gesture, occurring wholly at the outer, vaikharī-like level, was never guaranteed to inherit properties that belong to the inner stages' relationship to sound.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: independent scriptural grounding for treating sphoṭa's three features as properties of the paśyantī–madhyamā–vaikharī relationship specifically, which is exactly why Ch. 10–15 can fail two of the three features for gesture without contradicting the tradition — gesture was extended from vaikharī's outer function, not from the inner manifestational relationship sphoṭa depends on.

Acoustic

Feature (a) maps onto speech as a temporally ordered stream of discrete articulatory-acoustic events, not a sustained tone.

Neuroscience

Feature (b) maps onto categorical perception — a phoneme category is grasped as a gestalt, not assembled from acoustic sub-cues.

Biosynthesis

Feature (c) is like asking whether a biological signal is an inevitable byproduct of structure, or an evolved, arbitrary code — like the genetic code's arbitrary codon mapping.

New Cluster · RQ 5Regional and Textual Variation in the Hasta Inventory

RQ4 (Ch. 17–24) treats "the hasta inventory" as a single fixed set. It isn't. This cluster tests whether that simplification threatens the earlier findings, or leaves them intact.

5.1 Two Base Inventories, Not One

The Nāṭyaśāstra itself documents 24 asaṃyuta (single-hand) hastas. Nandikeśvara's later Abhinaya Darpaṇa documents 28 — the version most widely taught today, opening with its own enumeration verse:

पताकस्त्रिपताकोऽर्धपताकः कर्तरीमुखः । मयूराख्योऽर्धचन्द्रश्च अरालः शुकतुण्डकः ॥ मुष्टिश्च शिखराख्यश्च कपित्थः कटकामुखः । सूचीचन्द्रकला पद्मकोशः सर्पशिरस्तथा ॥ मृगशीर्षः सिंहमुखः कङ्गूलश्चालपद्मकः । चतुरो भ्रमरश्चैव हंसास्यो हंसपक्षकः ॥ सन्दंशो मुकुलश्चैव ताम्रचूडस्त्रिशूलकः । इत्यसंयुतहस्तानामष्टाविंशतिरीरिता ॥

5.2 What Changes Between the Two Texts, and What Doesn't

The divergence is specific and documentable, not vague. The Nāṭyaśāstra includes Ūrṇanābha, which the Abhinaya Darpaṇa drops entirely. The Abhinaya Darpaṇa in turn adds five hastas the Nāṭyaśāstra doesn't name at all: Ardhapatāka, Mayūra, Candrakalā, Siṃhamukha, and Triśūla. Of the hastas both texts do share, several carry the same name but a measurably different hand-shape or a different assigned meaning between the two sources — Nandikeśvara is refining, not simply copying, Bharata.

5.3 Living Traditions Diverge Further

Bharatanatyam, Kathakali, Odissi, Kuchipudi, Manipuri, and Mohiniattam all trace their hasta vocabularies back to this same textual base — but each performance tradition has, over centuries of independent regional transmission, settled on its own preferred subset, its own preferred hand-shapes for shared names, and in some cases its own additional gestures documented only in regional manuals outside the two root texts. This is exactly what you'd expect of a living oral-pedagogical tradition descending from a shared written source: the root inventory is stable in outline, variable in detail, the way a language's core grammar is stable while its dialects diverge in pronunciation and idiom.

5.4 What the Divergence Means for the Vaikharī-Extension Argument

Acoustic

Textual and regional divergence in a sign inventory is exactly what dialectology expects of any transmitted phonological system — Sanskrit itself has regional pronunciation traditions (śikṣā variation) without that undermining the claim that a stable phonemic system exists.

Neuroscience

A category system tolerating boundary redrawing at the margins while keeping a stable core is the normal profile of categorical perception generally — category boundaries are known to be somewhat malleable with training and context, not razor-fixed.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to a conserved gene family that has undergone lineage-specific duplication and loss — the core function is conserved even where individual family members differ across species.

Result for RQ4 and RQ6: the divergence documented here does not overturn Ch. 17–24's finding that the inventory is finite, digital, and closed — it refines it. "Finite and closed" was always claimed at the level of a given text or tradition, never as a single universal list; RQ5 makes that scope condition explicit rather than leaving it implicit.

New Cluster · RQ 9Two-Hand Coordination: The Saṃyukta Hasta Layer

Everything tested in RQ1–RQ8 above (Ch. 1–40) was argued from the asaṃyukta (single-hand) inventory. That is a real limitation, not a rounding error: a system built from one hand acting alone is a different compositional object than a system that lets two hands combine. This cluster asks the coordination question one level up — not speech-plus-gesture (RQ3), but hand-plus-hand within gesture itself — using the second half of the hasta inventory the earlier chapters set aside.

9.1 The Textual Count Problem, Reported Honestly

Before any structural claim, the count itself needs to be stated as contested, because it is. The Nāṭyaśāstra is standardly cited as naming 13 saṃyukta hastas; the Abhinaya Darpaṇa expands this to 23 (sometimes given as 24, depending on how a boundary case is counted in a given edition); the later Nartananirnaya gives 17. Three source texts, three different numbers, for what is nominally the same category. This is not a defect specific to saṃyukta hastas — RQ5 above documented the same kind of divergence for the asaṃyukta set (24 vs. 28) — but it is worth restating here explicitly rather than picking one number and presenting it as settled, because the saṃyukta count varies proportionally more across sources than the asaṃyukta count does.

Acoustic

A category whose boundary is drawn differently by different transmitters, while the core members stay stable, is the ordinary signature of a dialect-bearing phonological inventory, not evidence the category itself is fictitious.

Neuroscience

Category-boundary disagreement between trained observers, with the prototypical members agreed on by all, is the standard finding in categorical-perception research at a category's edges — disagreement concentrates at boundaries, not at cores.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to different taxonomic authorities disagreeing on species-count within a genus while agreeing on the genus's defining traits — a classification dispute, not a claim that the genus doesn't exist.

9.3 Testing the Asymmetric Subtype Against RQ4's Productivity Bar

RQ4 (Ch. 19–21) found the asaṃyukta inventory non-productive: individual hastas mostly signify directly, and sequences combine by audience-inferred concatenation rather than generative rule. The asymmetric saṃyukta subtype is the one real candidate in the whole hasta system for something closer to true composition — two independently named, independently meaningful units (say, a Śikhara hand and a flat palm) combining into a compound sign (Śivaliṅga) whose meaning is not simply the sum or sequence of its parts but a specific, third, culturally fixed referent.

The test has to be precise about what would count as passing. Genuine combinatorial productivity, in the sense Ch. 30's design-features checklist requires, means the rule for combining is general and can generate meanings not individually pre-listed — the way syntax generates sentences never previously heard. What the saṃyukta inventory actually documents is different: each asymmetric compound is itself a separately named, separately taught, closed-list entry — Śivaliṅga is taught as its own item, not derived on the fly from a general two-hand combination rule the way a Sanskrit sentence is derived from general syntax. A student learns "Śikhara-hand-plus-flat-palm = Śivaliṅga" the same way they learn any single hasta's name and meaning — the compounding happened once, historically, and was then fixed and listed, rather than being available as a live, general productive operation a performer or audience could apply to unlisted combinations.

Acoustic

This is the same distinction phonology draws between a productive morphological rule (any new noun takes a regular plural) and a lexicalized compound (a fixed idiom whose parts no longer combine freely with anything else) — saṃyukta hastas pattern with the latter.

Neuroscience

Parallels the documented distinction between rule-based combinatorial processing and item-based lexical retrieval — a fixed compound is retrieved as a whole from declarative memory, not assembled online from a general rule each time it's produced.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to a fixed protein complex assembled from two named subunits in one specific, evolutionarily fixed configuration, versus a truly combinatorial system like antibody diversity, which generates novel combinations on the fly from a general recombination rule.

FindingVerdict
Does the asymmetric saṃyukta subtype rescue RQ4's non-productivity finding?No — closed-list compounding, not generative combination

This is, again, a genuinely informative negative result rather than a deflationary one: it is precisely the two-hand layer where a productivity claim would have been most plausible, and testing it directly — rather than assuming RQ4's single-hand finding simply carries over untested — is what makes the negative finding here worth reporting at all.

9.4 What the Saṃyukta Layer Adds to the Coordination Argument (RQ3)

Ch. 26–28 argued that gesture is constitutively coordinated with the other three abhinaya channels (vācika, āhārya, sāttvika), not independent of them. The saṃyukta layer shows a second, internal coordination running alongside that external one: within the bodily (āṅgika) channel itself, the two hands are coordinated with each other, not two independent single-hand signal streams running in parallel. Añjali's meaning depends on both hands being symmetric; Śivaliṅga's meaning depends on the specific asymmetric pairing. Neither hand's shape is independently interpretable as carrying the compound's meaning on its own — evidence that the coordination principle Ch. 26–28 established at the level of the four abhinaya channels also operates one level down, within the gestural channel's own bilateral structure.

Añjali — अञ्जलि — "Salutation"Symmetric-bilateral construction

LakṣaṇaThe left hand held flat and cupped, palm upward; the right hand in Śikhara (fisted, thumb raised) placed above it.

PrakriyāFunctions as a container-schema gesture — its compositional logic is spatial (an enclosed space between two matching hands) rather than combinatorial (two different named shapes producing a third meaning).

UdāharaṇaDocumented uses include a box or casket, concealment, a covered vessel, and — in narrative mime — holding a small enclosed object such as a jewel or bird.

PhalaA useful third data point for the symmetric category: unlike Añjali and Kapota, its meaning depends on the enclosed spatial relation between the hands, not merely on the repeated hand-shape — showing that "symmetric" hastas are not all structurally identical to each other either, even though none of them pass 9.3's productivity test.

Combination alone isn't the bar; a productive rule is. A small, fixed, closed list of one-off compounds (Śivaliṅga, Garuḍa, Nāgabandha) is structurally different from a rule that could, in principle, combine any two asaṃyukta shapes and have the result be interpretable by a naive observer applying the rule for the first time. Nothing in the documented saṃyukta tradition works that second way — each compound had to be individually taught and fixed historically, the same way each single hasta was.

Not for the hastas actually used as case studies here. Añjali, Kapota, and Śivaliṅga appear, under those names and with substantially the same construction, across the Nāṭyaśāstra-descended tradition regardless of which text's total count you're using — the disagreement concentrates at the inventory's outer boundary (which rarer, more regionally specific compounds count as "in" the list), not at its well-attested core, exactly as 9.1's acoustic-lens analogy notes.

It sharpens the conclusion rather than changing it. The white paper argues gesture is conventionally coded, coordinated, and dhvani-style rather than sphoṭa-style or fully generative. RQ9 tests the one remaining structural objection — that two-hand combination might supply the missing productivity — directly, on its own terms, and reports that it doesn't. That is additional, independently gathered support for the same conclusion, not a revision of it.

New Cluster · RQ 10The Untested Fourth Channel: Sāttvika Bhāva

Ch. 26 (mūla śloka) named all four abhinaya channels — āṅgika, vācika, āhārya, sāttvika — at the point of definition, and Ch. 27–29 tested coordination between āṅgika and vācika in detail. But sāttvika itself was never separately tested against the same evidentiary bar RQ2 and RQ6 applied to āṅgika. That is a real gap, because sāttvika is not a minor channel: Abhinavagupta's own commentary treats it as the channel closest to rasa itself. This cluster closes the gap by running the identical tests — conventionality, sign-type, dhvani-fit — against a channel that is, on its face, the opposite of āṅgika in one crucial respect: it is involuntary, not trained.

10.1 What Sāttvika Claims, Restated at the Same Precision as Ch. 1

The Nāṭyaśāstra defines sāttvika bhāva as arising from sattva — a state of undistracted mental absorption — and manifesting as involuntary psychophysical states that overtake the performer when genuinely absorbed in the emotion being portrayed, rather than states the performer deliberately assembles gesture by gesture. This is a structurally different kind of claim than the aupacārika prayoga argument tested in Ch. 1–8. That argument extended vaikharī's externalization-for-another property to a trained, codified sign system. Sāttvika bhāva, on the tradition's own account, is not trained in the same sense at all — a performer is taught to cultivate the conditions under which it arises (through sustained absorption, or abhyāsa), but the specific bodily events themselves — tears, gooseflesh, a breaking voice — are described as arising involuntarily once that absorption is genuine, not as individually rehearsed and deployed the way a hasta is.

Neuroscience

Biosynthesis

10.2 Testing Sāttvika Against Ch. 12's Conventionality Criterion — And Getting the Opposite Answer

Ch. 12 found that āṅgika fails sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship and instead satisfies saṅketa (convention): a hasta's meaning must be explicitly taught, name by name. Sāttvika bhāva, tested on the same criterion, gives the opposite result for the paradigm case. Tears arising from genuine grief are not a taught, arbitrary pairing between a shape and a meaning — they are naturally, universally, cross-culturally associated with grief, in a way that does not require saṅketa to be intelligible even to an untrained observer. This is not a minor variation on āṅgika's finding; it is a structurally different position on the same test.

This has an immediate and important consequence for the sphoṭa-versus-dhvani argument built across Ch. 9–16 and Ch. 37–39. That argument was built and is valid specifically for āṅgika. It was never claimed, and should not be assumed, to extend automatically to sāttvika — a channel whose paradigm cases are naturally rather than conventionally linked to their meaning is, if anything, closer to sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship than āṅgika ever was, precisely on the feature (c) test that was decisive against āṅgika in Ch. 12.

10.3 The Complication: Staged Sāttvika and the Lokadharmī/Nāṭyadharmī Line

This finding needs one immediate qualification, because performance practice complicates the "naturally manifested" reading. A trained performer does not, in most staged contexts, wait for genuine autonomic grief before producing stage-tears; skilled performers are documented as able to produce a controlled approximation of sāttvika states — a controlled vaivarṇya, a summoned tremor — through technique. This is precisely the lokadharmī/nāṭyadharmī distinction Ch. 33 already established: sāttvika bhāva has a naturalistic register (lokadharmī — the real thing, arising involuntarily) and a stylized, technically produced register (nāṭyadharmī — a trained simulation of the real thing for staging purposes).

Once staged, technically-produced sāttvika is, in an important sense, re-conventionalized — a trained performer's controlled tremor is closer to a codified sign than to the spontaneous original it represents. The honest finding is therefore register-dependent rather than a single flat verdict: genuine, lokadharmī sāttvika bhāva is naturally linked to its meaning and sits closer to sphoṭa's manifestation relationship; staged, nāṭyadharmī sāttvika bhāva is a trained approximation and sits closer to āṅgika's conventionalized profile. Both registers are legitimate and both are named in the tradition's own vocabulary — this cluster's contribution is making explicit which register each specific claim about sāttvika bhāva applies to.

Acoustic

Directly parallel to the documented distinction in voice-quality research between genuine emotional vocal perturbation and an actor's trained simulation of it — acoustic analysis can sometimes, though not always, distinguish the two, exactly the register split this section names.

Neuroscience

Maps onto documented findings that trained affect-simulation (actors producing convincing facial expressions on cue) recruits voluntary motor-cortical pathways alongside, and sometimes partially substituting for, the involuntary limbic pathways engaged during genuine affect.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to an engineered cell line producing a molecule that is structurally identical to one made by a natural constitutive pathway — same output, different control mechanism, and the difference matters for how the system should be modeled.

10.4 Sāttvika as Index Rather Than Symbol — A Peircean Sharpening

The Cross-Tradition Comparison table above (Peirce's icon/index/symbol trichotomy) already noted that this tradition discusses resemblance, convention, and manifestation without formally trichotomizing them the way Peirce does. Sāttvika bhāva is exactly the case where that formal Peircean vocabulary earns its keep. Genuine, lokadharmī sāttvika bhāva is best classified as an index — a sign causally produced by the state it signifies, the way smoke indexes fire — rather than as a symbol in Peirce's sense, which is a sign conventionally, arbitrarily paired with its meaning (the category āṅgika was shown to belong to in Ch. 12). Staged, nāṭyadharmī sāttvika bhāva, by contrast, drifts toward symbolic status precisely because it is trained and reproduced on cue rather than causally triggered.

This sharpens rather than undermines the earlier chapters' findings: it confirms that the tradition's own materials already contain, in nascent form, exactly the distinction Peirce later formalized — a genuine convergence worth noting for the same reason Ch. 31's sign-language benchmark and Ch. 37's dhvani argument were both worth noting, as independent external confirmation of a distinction this tradition draws in its own terms.

10.5 Does Sāttvika Fit Dhvani, or Does It Need Its Own Model?

Ch. 37–39 proposed dhvani as the better-fitting model for āṅgika specifically because sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship failed for āṅgika's general case. Sāttvika bhāva presents close to the reverse situation: on 10.2's finding, genuine sāttvika bhāva is closer to a manifestation-relationship than āṅgika ever was. This means the dhvani-over-sphoṭa argument should not be extended to genuine, lokadharmī sāttvika bhāva without separate justification — doing so would repeat exactly the error Ch. 4 warned against: assuming a property established for one case transfers automatically to a structurally different one.

That said, dhvani retains real purchase even here, for a narrower reason. Rasa theory's own technical vocabulary treats sāttvika bhāva as one of the strongest vyañjakas (suggestors) of rasa — arguably the strongest of the four abhinaya channels for this specific purpose, because an audience's qualified perception (sahṛdaya) of a performer's genuine or convincingly staged tremor is precisely what dhvani theory calls suggestion rather than direct statement: the tremor does not denote grief the way a word does, it evokes grief in a prepared perceiver. So the finding is layered rather than contradictory: sāttvika's sign-meaning relationship (indexical/natural) is not well modeled by dhvani, but sāttvika's function within a rasa-producing performance (evoking, not stating) is a dhvani-style operation. These are two different questions, and this cluster's contribution is separating them explicitly rather than letting one answer stand in for both.

Stambha — स्तम्भ — "Paralysis / Stupefaction"First of the eight, Nāṭyaśāstra 6.22

LakṣaṇaA sudden cessation of voluntary movement — the body freezes, unresponsive to ordinary stimuli, for the duration of the overwhelming state.

PrakriyāPhysiologically, this is documented in the tradition's own descriptive terms as an overwhelming of ordinary motor control by an intense emotional state — closer to what contemporary affect science would call a freeze response than to any deliberately assumed posture. It is the clearest single case for 10.4's indexical classification: the stillness is caused by the state, not chosen to represent it.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include the moment a character receives shattering news, the instant before a fatal recognition scene (pratyabhijñāna), and sustained astonishment (vismaya) at a divine or supernatural apparition.

PhalaThe paradigm negative case for a sphoṭa-manifestation reading being rejected the way Ch. 12 rejected it for āṅgika: here it is the opposite — stambha's meaning is about as close to a natural, non-arbitrary sign-meaning relationship as this entire document's case-study set contains.

LakṣaṇaVisible sweating arising under emotional rather than thermal or exertive load.

PrakriyāAn autonomic response the tradition explicitly separates from thermal sweating by context — the same physiological event, read differently depending on the surrounding dramatic circumstance, which is itself a documented case of context-dependent interpretation operating on a naturally-produced sign rather than a conventionally-coded one.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include acute fear (bhaya), the anxious anticipation preceding a high-stakes confrontation, and the physical symptoms of romantic longing (a documented convention within śṛṅgāra rasa portrayal).

PhalaA useful test of 10.3's register distinction: sveda is among the harder sāttvika bhāvas to produce convincingly on cue, which is exactly why performance manuals document specific technical methods for approximating it — direct evidence that staged sāttvika is recognized within the tradition itself as requiring trained technique, not simply waiting for the real thing.

Romāñca — रोमाञ्च — "Horripilation"Third of the eight

LakṣaṇaGooseflesh; the hairs of the body standing on end.

PrakriyāAmong the most involuntary and least performer-controllable of the eight — a piloerection response that resists deliberate production far more than, say, tears do, making it a limit case for 10.3's staged/genuine distinction: nāṭyadharmī romāñca is documented as often relying on external aids or context-framing rather than pure technique.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include the joy of reunion after long separation, the thrill of witnessing extraordinary valor, and religious awe (bhakti-inflected wonder) at a divine manifestation.

PhalaThe strongest single case for a genuinely cross-cultural, non-conventional sign: romāñca as a marker of intense positive affect is documented across performance traditions with no shared textual lineage, consistent with 10.2's finding that this channel does not depend on saṅketa the way āṅgika does.

Svarabheda — स्वरभेद — "Breaking of the Voice"Fourth of the eight

LakṣaṇaA cracking, choking, or otherwise involuntarily disrupted vocal quality overtaking ordinary speech.

PrakriyāStructurally distinct from every other sāttvika bhāva in this list because it operates on the vācika channel rather than purely on āṅgika's bodily one — direct evidence, at the level of a single bhāva, that the four abhinaya channels are not cleanly separable even in principle; svarabheda is sāttvika content delivered through the vācika channel's own medium.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include grief-stricken lament (karuṇa rasa), a confession delivered under extreme emotional strain, and the choked speech preceding a character's collapse into weeping.

PhalaDirect evidence against treating the fourfold abhinaya scheme (Ch. 26) as four cleanly separable parallel channels: svarabheda shows sāttvika bhāva can be realized through vācika rather than alongside it, a finer-grained coordination than Ch. 27's channel-level reading alone captures.

LakṣaṇaInvoluntary shaking of the limbs or entire body.

PrakriyāAmong the more technically trainable of the eight — performance manuals document specific controlled-trembling techniques, making vepathu a useful midpoint case between stambha (barely trainable) and a fully codified hasta (entirely trained): it sits on a documented gradient of trainability rather than a sharp binary.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include acute terror (bhayānaka rasa), extreme cold as a naturalistic (lokadharmī) cue, and the physical agitation preceding a violent confrontation (raudra rasa).

PhalaBest single case for 10.3's central claim that trainability is a gradient, not a binary between "natural sāttvika" and "conventional āṅgika" — vepathu sits measurably between the two poles this cluster's table (10.2) treats as endpoints.

Vaivarṇya — वैवर्ण्य — "Change of Color"Sixth of the eight

LakṣaṇaA visible change in facial complexion — pallor, flushing, or an ashen quality — arising with strong emotion.

PrakriyāPhysiologically grounded in autonomic vasodilation/vasoconstriction, and documented in performance manuals as one of the sāttvika bhāvas most dependent on lighting, makeup, and staging convention (āhārya) to be visible to an audience at all — a direct, concrete case of one abhinaya channel (sāttvika) depending on another (āhārya) for its communicative effect to reach the audience.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include the pallor of fear, the flush of anger, and the specific ashen quality associated with encountering the uncanny or supernatural (bhayānaka and adbhuta rasa).

PhalaDirect textual evidence that the fourfold coordination principle (Ch. 26–28) operates even at the level of a single bhāva's basic legibility — vaivarṇya is a case where sāttvika's communicative function is documented as literally dependent on āhārya's staging support, not merely coordinated with it in the loose sense Ch. 27 described for vācika.

Aśru — अश्रु — "Tears"Seventh of the eight

LakṣaṇaWeeping — tears arising from the eyes under emotional load, whether sorrowful or, in a documented secondary use, from overwhelming joy.

PrakriyāThe single most extensively documented sāttvika bhāva in the technical performance literature, precisely because it is both highly legible to an audience and only moderately trainable — manuals document specific gaze-fixation and breath techniques for inducing it on cue, alongside frank acknowledgment that genuine tears remain the more convincing case whenever a performer can summon them honestly.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts span the widest range of any sāttvika bhāva: grief (karuṇa), devotional ecstasy (bhakti-inflected rasa), and, notably, joy at reunion — aśru is one of the few sāttvika bhāvas the tradition documents as legitimately ambiguous between two opposed sthāyibhāvas without contextual disambiguation always being possible.

PhalaThe single strongest case study anywhere in this document for dhvani's context-dependent polysemy (Ch. 38) operating on a naturally-produced, non-conventional sign rather than a conventionally-coded one — extending 10.5's finding that dhvani describes sāttvika's function even where it does not describe its sign-type.

Pralaya — प्रलय — "Fainting / Collapse"Eighth and final of the eight

LakṣaṇaTotal loss of consciousness or complete physical collapse under overwhelming emotional intensity.

PrakriyāThe terminal point of the sāttvika sequence in both a structural and a dramaturgical sense — listed last in the enumeration verse, and documented as the most intense point on the involuntary-response scale the other seven bhāvas populate. Almost entirely simulated rather than genuinely triggered in performance for obvious practical reasons, making it the clearest case of nāṭyadharmī sāttvika fully substituting for lokadharmī sāttvika.

UdāharaṇaDocumented dramatic contexts include the climactic collapse following catastrophic news, ecstatic collapse in intense devotional (bhakti) portrayal, and the culmination of a sustained buildup through the other seven bhāvas in sequence within a single scene.

PhalaThe clearest limit case for 10.3's register distinction: pralaya is, in ordinary performance practice, almost always nāṭyadharmī rather than lokadharmī — which means the "sāttvika is naturally, not conventionally, linked to its meaning" finding (10.2) applies to this bhāva's paradigm, off-stage case far more cleanly than to its typical on-stage realization, exactly the qualification 10.3 exists to state honestly rather than let the general finding overclaim.

New Cluster · RQ 11Cross-Cultural Benchmark: Testing Universality Against Independent Gestural Systems

Ch. 31 used natural sign-language linguistics as an external check on one specific claim: that channel, not documented structure, is what determines linguistic status. This cluster extends the same benchmarking discipline to a different question the earlier chapters left open — whether the specific structural findings reached for the hasta system (digital categorization, low productivity, concatenative rather than generative combination, register-dependent naturalism) are a general property of codified performance-gesture systems, or a peculiar feature of this one textual tradition. Three independently developed, textually unrelated systems are tested against the same criteria: Japanese Noh kata, Balinese/Javanese mudra-and-mask vocabularies, and the 19th-century Western Delsarte system together with Laban Movement Analysis.

11.1 Japanese Noh Kata: A Independently Developed Codified-Gesture Tradition

Noh theatre's kata — a fixed vocabulary of named, stylized movement patterns — developed from the 14th century onward under Zeami and his successors, with no documented textual contact with the Sanskrit dramaturgical tradition. If the structural findings reached above for the hasta system are artifacts of this one textual lineage, Noh kata would be expected to look structurally different. If instead they reflect a more general property of how codified performance-gesture systems tend to organize themselves, Noh kata should show a comparable profile.

The comparison holds up on several of the tested dimensions. Kata are individually named, taught through direct imitation of a master (a closed, non-generative pedagogical method closely resembling how hastas are documented as taught), and combined within a performance according to convention and narrative context rather than a generative combinatorial grammar — the same concatenative-not-generative profile Ch. 20–21 documented for hasta sequences. Noh's own aesthetic vocabulary distinguishes jo-ha-kyū (a structuring principle of gradual intensification) as governing how kata are sequenced across a performance, which functions similarly to how nāṭyadharmī convention governs hasta sequencing — a convention-governed ordering principle, not a syntax in the linguistic sense.

11.2 Balinese and Javanese Mudra-and-Mask Systems

Balinese topeng (masked dance-drama) and Javanese wayang wong combine a codified hand-gesture vocabulary with masks that themselves carry fixed conventional meaning — structurally close to the āhārya-āṅgika coordination already documented for the Sanskrit tradition (10.2's vaivarṇya case study above is a direct parallel: a bodily sign depending on a costume/staging element for full legibility). Balinese performance vocabulary, like the Sanskrit hasta system, is documented as regionally variable across villages and lineages even within Bali itself — directly paralleling RQ5's finding (§5) that regional and textual divergence is the expected profile of a living, orally-transmitted codified sign system rather than evidence against the system's coherence.

One genuine point of difference is worth reporting honestly rather than smoothing over: some documented Balinese mask-gesture combinations are read by trained audiences as carrying more context-independent, stable meaning than the Sanskrit hasta system's own polysemous items (like Pātāka, case-studied above) — suggesting that the degree of context-dependent polysemy dhvani theory is built to accommodate (Ch. 38) may not be a universal feature of all codified gestural systems, but rather a specific characteristic of how the Sanskrit tradition's inventory happens to be structured. This is a genuine limitation of the dhvani-over-sphoṭa proposal's generalizability beyond the source tradition, and it is reported here rather than left implicit.

11.3 Delsarte and Laban: A Western Attempt at the Same Project

François Delsarte's 19th-century system for codifying expressive gesture, and Rudolf Laban's 20th-century Effort/Shape framework for movement analysis, represent an independently developed Western attempt to systematize exactly the phenomenon this document has been testing in the Sanskrit tradition — codified, meaningful, trainable gesture. Neither system engaged the Sanskrit material directly. The comparison is instructive less for structural agreement than for a difference in ambition worth naming precisely: Delsarte's system aimed at a claimed universal, near-natural correspondence between specific body zones and specific emotional categories — a stronger manifestational claim than this document found the Sanskrit hasta system could sustain (Ch. 12's decisive negative finding).

Laban's Effort/Shape framework, by contrast, deliberately avoids fixed sign-meaning pairings altogether, instead describing movement along continuous qualitative dimensions (weight, time, space, flow) — structurally closer to sāttvika bhāva's graded, non-digital profile (10.2 above) than to āṅgika's digital, categorically-taught hasta inventory. The three-way comparison (Sanskrit hasta / Delsarte / Laban) therefore maps onto a real spectrum from strongly digital-conventional to strongly continuous-natural, with the Sanskrit tradition's own two channels — āṅgika and sāttvika — occupying two different points on that same spectrum, a finding RQ10 already established from within the tradition and RQ11 now confirms from outside it.

11.4 What Four Independent Systems Converging Actually Establishes

Four textually unrelated traditions — Sanskrit, Japanese, Balinese/Javanese, and 20th-century Western movement analysis — converge on the same structural split this document derived from the Sanskrit sources alone: codified performance-gesture systems tend toward digital, non-generative, convention-governed organization, while involuntary or deliberately non-coded expressive movement tends toward continuous, graded organization. This convergence is genuine evidence for a general claim, but it should be stated at the same precision the rest of this document insists on: it supports the claim that this structural split is a common feature of how humans organize codified performance-gesture systems, not the stronger claim that the Sanskrit tradition's specific findings (the exact degree of polysemy, the specific dhvani-based model) generalize word-for-word to every other tradition. §11.2's honest report of Balinese mask-gesture's lower polysemy is the concrete reminder that the general structural pattern and the specific theoretical model built to explain one tradition's version of it are two different levels of claim.

Acoustic

Four independently evolved languages showing the same broad phonological universals (a small closed consonant-vowel inventory, hierarchical syllable structure) is treated in linguistics as strong evidence for a general cognitive constraint — without implying any two languages share identical phoneme inventories or phonotactic rules. The gestural parallel here is exactly this pattern.

Neuroscience

Parallels cross-cultural facial-expression research (building on Ekman's cross-cultural studies) finding a shared core of recognizable affect displays alongside genuine, documented cultural "display rules" governing when and how intensely they are shown — a shared substrate with real, non-trivial cultural variation layered on top.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to convergent evolution: unrelated lineages (birds and bats evolving flight independently) arriving at structurally similar solutions under similar functional pressures — the similarity is real and informative precisely because it arose without shared ancestry, not despite that fact.

New Cluster · RQ 12The Rasasūtra: Where Āṅgika and Sāttvika Both Terminate

Every earlier cluster has tested a channel or a mechanism in isolation — āṅgika's sign-type (RQ2, RQ6), its combinatorics (RQ4, RQ9), sāttvika's sign-type (RQ10), and cross-tradition structure (RQ11). None of them has yet asked the question the tradition itself treats as primary: what is all of this coordination for. The Nāṭyaśāstra's own answer is the rasasūtra — the single most debated verse in the entire Sanskrit dramaturgical tradition. This cluster closes the document's arc by showing how RQ1–RQ11's separate findings about āṅgika and sāttvika are, on Abhinavagupta's own reading, two coordinated routes to the same destination.

12.1 The Verse Itself, and Why It Generated Four Centuries of Rival Theories

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse — The Rasasūtra

विभावानुभावव्यभिचारिसंयोगाद्रसनिष्पत्तिः

vibhāvānubhāvavyabhicārisaṃyogād rasaniṣpattiḥ

Source: Nāṭyaśāstra 6.31 (numbering varies slightly by edition) — the single most extensively commented-on sūtra in the entire tradition, cited and re-glossed by nearly every major alaṃkāra-śāstra author from Bhaṭṭa Lollaṭa onward. Verify against the GOS or Kāvyamālā edition before citing the exact kārikā number.

Translation: Rasa is accomplished (niṣpatti) through the coming-together (saṃyoga) of the determinants (vibhāva), the consequents (anubhāva), and the transitory states (vyabhicāribhāva).

Prakriyā (how it works here): Note precisely which term is missing from this list: sāttvikabhāva is not named in the sūtra itself, even though Bharata treats it as one of the four constitutive abhinaya channels (Ch. 26) and devotes a full enumeration to it (RQ10's mūla śloka). This omission is exactly what generated centuries of commentarial dispute — is sāttvika bhāva folded into anubhāva (the visible consequents), or does it operate differently? RQ10's finding that sāttvika bhāva is structurally distinct from āṅgika (involuntary vs. trained, indexical vs. conventional) turns out to matter directly here: Abhinavagupta's own resolution, discussed in 12.3 below, depends on treating sāttvika bhāva as a privileged, especially reliable subtype of anubhāva — because its involuntary, non-conventional character (established independently in RQ10) makes it a more trustworthy sign of genuine inner state than a codified, conventionally-produced anubhāva like a hasta could ever be.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: the rasasūtra is where RQ2's sphoṭa-rejection, RQ6's linguistic-status finding, RQ10's indexical-sāttvika finding, and Ch. 37–39's dhvani proposal all have to answer to the same higher-order question at once — not whether gesture means something, but whether it can produce rasa, which the tradition treats as a stronger and different achievement than mere denotation.

12.2 Four Rival Theories of How the Sūtra's "Coming-Together" Actually Works

The commentarial tradition on this single sūtra produced (at minimum) four distinct theories of the mechanism by which vibhāva-anubhāva-vyabhicāribhāva combination produces rasa, each with direct consequences for how āṅgika and sāttvika should be understood as signs.

That is not a coincidence to smooth over; it means RQ1's finding and RQ12's finding come from the same authorial hand, applying the same underlying theoretical commitment (manifestation-for-a-qualified-perceiver) at two different levels of the system — once to how gesture relates to vaikharī, and once to how the whole performance relates to rasa.

12.3 Why Sāttvika Bhāva Is, on This Reading, the More Trustworthy Channel

Abhinavagupta's commentary on the rasasūtra gives sāttvika bhāva a privileged evidentiary status precisely because of the property RQ10 established independently: its involuntary, non-conventional production. A hasta can be produced by a performer with no corresponding inner state at all — nothing about producing Pātāka requires the performer to feel anything. A genuine tremor (vepathu) or genuine tears (aśru) are, by RQ10's own finding, much harder to produce without some corresponding absorption, which is exactly why Abhinavagupta treats sāttvika bhāva as the anubhāva category spectators find most convincing — not because it is more meaningful in the semantic sense RQ2–RQ9 tested, but because its causal, indexical relationship to inner state (established independently via Peirce in 10.4) makes it harder to fake and therefore more evidentially weighty within the rasa-production process specifically.

This produces a genuinely interesting asymmetry worth stating precisely: āṅgika is the more linguistically sophisticated channel by every measure RQ2–RQ9 tested (closed inventory, digital categorization, some combinatorial structure, conventionally taught), while sāttvika is the evidentially more trustworthy channel specifically for rasa-production, for the opposite reason — because it resists exactly the kind of deliberate, trainable manipulation that gives āṅgika its structure. The two channels are not competing for the same job; they do different, complementary work within the fourfold coordination Ch. 26 established, and RQ10–RQ12 together explain why the tradition needed both rather than treating one as a redundant elaboration of the other.

Acoustic

Parallels the documented distinction in forensic voice analysis between a controllable acoustic cue (word choice, prosodic emphasis) and a harder-to-fake one (micro-tremor under genuine stress) — the harder-to-fake signal is weighted more heavily as evidence precisely because it resists deliberate manipulation.

Neuroscience

Directly parallel to documented findings in deception research: involuntary autonomic leakage (micro-expressions, genuine pupil dilation) is treated as more diagnostic of true internal state than voluntarily controlled expressive behavior, for the identical reason Abhinavagupta gives sāttvika bhāva evidentiary priority.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to how a constitutive biomarker (one the organism cannot suppress on demand) is treated as more diagnostically reliable in a clinical assay than an inducible one that could in principle be triggered artificially — reliability tracks resistance to manipulation, not complexity of signal.

12.4 Closing the Loop: How RQ1 and RQ12 Meet

Ch. 1–8 established that abhinaya is licensed as vaikharī's further reach on one specific, narrow property: externalization-for-another. RQ12 closes the document's arc by showing what that externalization is ultimately in service of, on Abhinavagupta's own account: not communication in the narrow linguistic sense RQ2–RQ9 spent thirty-two chapters carefully bounding, but the manifestation of rasa in a prepared perceiver — a communicative act, yes, but one whose success condition is aesthetic relish (bhukti/āsvāda) rather than propositional understanding. This is precisely why the document's central finding — that gesture is dhvani-style suggestion rather than sphoṭa-style or fully linguistic denotation — was the theoretically appropriate model all along: dhvani theory and rasa theory are, in Abhinavagupta's own synthesis in the Locana, the same theoretical apparatus applied to poetry and to drama respectively. RQ12 is not a new argument bolted onto the first eleven; it is where they were always heading.

New Cluster · RQ 10Paśyantī and the Outer Limit of the Extension

Every cluster so far has tested how far codified gesture reaches outward — toward sphoṭa, toward productivity, toward sentence-level meaning. This cluster tests the opposite direction: how far the extension argument reaches inward, toward silence, stillness, and the pre-verbal stage the tradition calls paśyantī. If vaikharī's defining property is externalization-for-another, does that property have a floor — a point past which stillness is no longer externalizing anything, and the extension argument simply stops applying?

10.1 Why This Question Was Not Yet Asked

New Cluster · RQ 11The Sphoṭa Controversy Outside Bhartṛhari's Own School

RQ2 (Ch. 9–16) tested sphoṭa's three features against gesture and found two of them wanting. That test implicitly treated sphoṭa theory as settled ground within its own tradition — a fixed yardstick to measure gesture against. It was never settled ground. Sphoṭa was one of classical India's most contested theoretical claims, rejected outright by two entire schools of Sanskrit philosophy of language. This cluster examines that internal controversy directly, because it changes how confidently RQ2's negative finding should be read.

11.1 The Mīmāṃsā Rejection: Kumārila Bhaṭṭa's Counter-Model

Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, writing in the Mīmāṃsā tradition centuries after Bhartṛhari's foundational statement of sphoṭa theory, rejects the sphoṭa as an unnecessary posit. On the Mīmāṃsā account, the individual phonemes (varṇas) themselves — not some further, separately manifested unit — directly convey meaning through eternal, unauthored convention (nitya-śabda and autpattika-sambandha, the primordial, non-conventional connection between word and meaning the Mīmāṃsakas hold as foundational to Vedic authority). On this view, positing an additional sphoṭa "revealed by" the sequence of sounds is explanatorily redundant: if the sequence of varṇas can be shown to convey the meaning directly through memory-trace (saṃskāra) accumulated across the hearing of each successive sound, no further unified entity needs to be manifested at all.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

Source: A representative Mīmāṃsaka-style verse restating Kumārila's position from the Ślokavārttika's Sphoṭavāda section (Tantravārttika/Ślokavārttika tradition) — treat this as a paraphrase-verse in the tradition's own idiom rather than a verbatim, page-verified citation; check against Gaṅgānātha Jhā's translation of the Ślokavārttika before scholarly citation.

Why it matters here: This is not a peripheral objection — it is a direct rejection of the very entity RQ2 (Ch. 9–16) tested gesture against. If a major school within the same broader tradition denies sphoṭa exists at all, RQ2's finding that "gesture fails two of sphoṭa's three features" needs a qualifier: gesture fails two of the three features of a theoretical entity whose reality is itself disputed inside classical Sanskrit philosophy of language, not an uncontested fact about language that gesture then additionally fails to meet.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: RQ2's negative finding is reframed, not reversed. Gesture still fails the sphoṭa test on Bhartṛhari's own terms — but the document's confidence in why that failure matters should be tempered by the fact that sphoṭa's own status was a live dispute, not settled doctrine, even among Sanskrit grammarians' contemporaries.

11.2 The Naiyāyika Counter-Model: Sequential Memory Without a Manifested Unit

The Nyāya school's rejection runs on a related but distinct track. Where Mīmāṃsā emphasizes eternal word-meaning connection, the Naiyāyikas emphasize a straightforwardly causal-cum-cognitive account: the last-heard phoneme in a sequence, combined with the memory-impressions (saṃskāra) left by each phoneme heard before it, is sufficient to produce a unified cognition of meaning (padajñāna) — again without needing a further manifested sphoṭa entity as an intermediate step. Gaṅgeśa's later Navya-Nyāya treatment sharpens this into a fully causal model: the auditory cognition of the final phoneme, occurring in a mind already holding the saṃskāras of the prior phonemes, is the direct cause of word-cognition.

11.3 What the Controversy Changes About RQ2's Method, and What It Doesn't

It is worth being precise about scope here, in the spirit of the Method note this whole document opened with. The controversy does not undo RQ2's finding that gesture, tested against Bhartṛhari's specific three features, fails two of them. Both the Mīmāṃsā and Nyāya rejections concern whether sphoṭa exists as a linguistic entity at all — they are not alternative accounts on which gesture would pass. If anything, both rival schools' emphasis on sequential memory-accumulation (saṃskāra) makes gesture's failure of feature (a) — the absence of a discretely sequenced manifesting cause in a held configuration — look even more decisive: whichever camp is right about how spoken meaning arises, all three (Bhartṛhari's sphoṭa camp, Mīmāṃsā, and Nyāya) agree that spoken meaning-cognition depends on a temporally ordered sequence of discrete sound-events. A held hasta has no such sequence to offer under any of the three competing accounts.

Acoustic

Three rival theories of how a spectrogram's temporal structure yields a phoneme category would still all agree the spectrogram needs to have temporal structure in the first place — the controversy is about the mechanism, not about whether sequencing matters at all.

Neuroscience

Comparable to competing computational models of speech perception (TRACE, Shortlist, cohort models) disagreeing sharply on mechanism while all requiring a temporally unfolding input signal — the disagreement never extends to whether time-ordering matters.

Biosynthesis

Like competing kinetic models of an enzymatic cascade disagreeing about rate-limiting steps while agreeing the reaction is sequential rather than instantaneous — the controversy narrows mechanism, not the basic requirement of ordered steps.

11.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 11 Closed

The internal Sanskrit controversy over sphoṭa's very existence is real, well-documented, and worth stating plainly rather than leaving implicit. It reframes RQ2's finding as resting on a contested theoretical entity rather than settled fact — but because every rival account converges on the requirement RQ2 actually tested (a temporally sequenced string of sound-events as meaning's cause), gesture's failure of that requirement survives the controversy fully intact. This is the more honest, and ultimately the stronger, way to hold the finding: not "sphoṭa is true and gesture fails it," but "however classical Sanskrit philosophy of language explains spoken meaning, gesture's static, held signs do not meet the shared requirement all three schools agree spoken meaning depends on."

New Cluster · RQ 12Rasa-Dhvani Integration: Suggestion in the Service of Aesthetic Emotion

Ch. 37–39 nominated dhvani as the better-fitting model for how gesture carries meaning, largely on structural grounds: no dependence on discrete sequencing, built-in accommodation of polysemy, an explicit qualified-perceiver requirement. What that discussion left underdeveloped is dhvani's own primary purpose within Ānandavardhana's system — it was not proposed as a general semiotic mechanism first and an aesthetic theory second. It was proposed specifically to explain how rasa, aesthetic emotion, is evoked in a receptive audience. This cluster tests whether gesture's use of dhvani-style suggestion is best understood on those original, rasa-centered terms, rather than as a semiotic borrowing stripped of its aesthetic purpose.

12.1 Rasa-Dhvani as Ānandavardhana's Central, Not Incidental, Case

Ānandavardhana's threefold division of dhvani — vastu-dhvani (suggested factual content), alaṃkāra-dhvani (suggested figures of speech), and rasa-dhvani (suggested aesthetic emotion) — is not a flat taxonomy of equal-weight cases. The Dhvanyāloka treats rasa-dhvani as the culminating, most refined case: a suggested sense that cannot, even in principle, be paraphrased into direct statement without destroying the very aesthetic experience it was meant to produce. Stating "the audience feels karuṇa (pathos)" is not the same event as an audience actually experiencing karuṇa through a suggestively evoked scene. This is a stronger claim than vastu-dhvani or alaṃkāra-dhvani make, both of which suggest content that could, awkwardly, be paraphrased without total loss.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

काव्येष्वेव तु ये तेषामर्थाः सन्ति निबन्धनम् । वाच्यातिशयिनस्ते तु ध्वनिं रसमयं विदुः ॥

kāvyeṣv eva tu ye teṣām arthāḥ santi nibandhanam | vācyātiśayinas te tu dhvaniṃ rasamayaṃ viduḥ ||

Source: A representative restatement, in the idiom of the alaṃkāra-śāstra tradition, of the Dhvanyāloka's ranking of rasa-dhvani above vastu- and alaṃkāra-dhvani (compare Dhvanyāloka 2.4 and surrounding kārikās) — verify exact numbering against Ingalls/Masson/Patwardhan's edition before scholarly citation.

Translation: "But those meanings in poetry that exceed what is literally stated and form its very foundation — those the learned know as dhvani in its rasa-filled form."

Why it matters here: If rasa-dhvani is the tradition's own most refined case of suggestion, then modeling gesture's polysemy (Ch. 38's finding that one hasta carries several context-resolved senses) purely as a semantic-disambiguation mechanism undersells what the tradition itself thought dhvani was ultimately for. A hasta held during a karuṇa-rasa passage is not merely disambiguating between "grief" and "longing" the way a dictionary entry disambiguates homonyms — it is functioning, on the tradition's own terms, as one contributing factor (vibhāva/anubhāva, in Nāṭyaśāstra's own rasa-sūtra vocabulary) toward a rasa-experience that exceeds any single paraphrasable content.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: gesture's dhvani-style suggestion should be read, wherever it occurs inside a rasa-oriented performance context, as serving an aesthetic-emotional function first and a narrower semantic-disambiguation function second — a refinement of Ch. 38's claim, not a contradiction of it.

12.2 Testing the Refinement Against a Documented Case

Pātāka's polysemy (Ch. 38's central example, revisited in the Case Studies section above: flag, cloud, forest, denial, water, wind) illustrates the refinement cleanly. In a purely narrative-descriptive passage — a performer miming "clouds gathering" — Pātāka's suggested sense functions close to vastu-dhvani: a suggested factual content, paraphrasable without much loss ("it means clouds"). But the same hasta, held during a passage building toward bhaya-rasa (fear) as storm-clouds gather ominously before a calamity, functions closer to rasa-dhvani: what is suggested is not simply "clouds" but a contribution to a building dread that cannot be reduced to the word "clouds" without losing the aesthetic point entirely.

12.3 Why This Matters for the Coordination Finding (RQ3)

This refinement also sharpens RQ3's coordination finding (Ch. 25–32). If gesture's higher-order function, in rasa-oriented contexts, is to contribute toward a rasa the audience experiences through the coordinated action of all four abhinaya channels together (āṅgika, vācika, āhārya, sāttvika), then coordination is not merely a structural fact about how the four channels happen to co-occur — it is functionally necessary, because rasa-dhvani's very definition (an aesthetic sense exceeding literal statement) requires multiple simultaneous channels for that "exceeding" to have anything to exceed. A single isolated channel has less to work with; sustained coordination across channels is what gives dhvani room to operate in its fullest, rasa-oriented sense.

Acoustic

Comparable to how a chord's emotional color exceeds what any single constituent note conveys — the "excess beyond the literal" that rasa-dhvani names has a direct acoustic analogue in how simultaneous frequencies combine into a perceptual whole irreducible to its parts.

Neuroscience

Parallels documented multisensory emotion-processing research, where congruent affective cues across modalities (facial expression, prosody, gesture) produce a stronger, qualitatively richer emotional read than any single channel alone — a superadditive effect consistent with rasa-dhvani's own claim that the suggested whole exceeds any paraphrasable part.

Biosynthesis

Like a signaling cascade where the physiological outcome (say, a coordinated stress response) depends on several hormones acting in concert, such that no single hormone's isolated effect predicts the combined, qualitatively distinct systemic outcome.

12.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 12 Closed

Dhvani-over-sphoṭa (Ch. 37–39) is confirmed and refined, not merely repeated, by attending to dhvani's own internal hierarchy. Gesture's suggested meanings are best modeled specifically as contributions toward rasa — the tradition's own most demanding case of suggestion — in performance contexts where rasa is the governing aim, and as simpler vastu-dhvani in more narrowly descriptive passages. This distinction was available in the source texts all along but had not yet been drawn out as its own tested claim.

New Cluster · RQ 13The Sāttvika Bhāvas Tested Against the Same Bars Applied to Āṅgika

RQ1–RQ12 test āṅgika (bodily/gestural expression) almost exclusively. The Nāṭyaśāstra's fourfold division (Ch. 26's shloka box) names a fourth channel — sāttvika, involuntary psychophysical states — that has been mentioned as a coordination partner throughout but never independently tested against the same evidentiary bars (sphoṭa-style manifestation, productivity, conventional-vs-natural coding) this document applied so carefully to hasta. This cluster closes that gap.

13.1 The Documented Inventory: Eight Sāttvika Bhāvas

The Nāṭyaśāstra names eight sāttvika bhāvas, involuntary psychophysical responses an actor is trained to produce convincingly on cue: stambha (paralysis/freezing), sveda (perspiration), romāñca (horripilation, hair standing on end), svarabheda (voice-breaking), vepathu (trembling), vaivarṇya (change of complexion/pallor), aśru (tears), and pralaya (fainting/loss of composure). Unlike the hasta inventory, these are not named hand-configurations but named psychophysiological states — the actor's task is not to hold a discrete shape but to convincingly produce a bodily response ordinarily involuntary.

13.2 Testing Feature (c) First: Is Sāttvika Convention-Governed or Natural?

This is the most interesting reversal in the whole document. Feature (c) (Ch. 12) was decisive and negative for hasta: gesture-meaning pairings are documented as taught name-by-name, evidence of convention rather than natural manifestation. For sāttvika bhāvas, the relationship runs the other way. Tears, trembling, and pallor are not arbitrary, conventionally-assigned signs for grief or fear the way a hasta's shape is an arbitrary sign for "lotus." They are the same physiological responses that occur in ordinary, unstylized experience of those emotions — the actor's craft lies in convincingly reproducing a naturally-occurring response on cue, not in learning an arbitrary code. This means sāttvika bhāvas pass a version of the naturalness test hasta failed, but for exactly that reason they raise a new question RQ13.3 addresses: does natural resemblance actually strengthen or weaken their claim to being part of a semiotic system at all?

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

स्तम्भः स्वेदोऽथ रोमाञ्चः स्वरभेदोऽथ वेपथुः । वैवर्ण्यमश्रु प्रलय इत्यष्टौ सात्त्विकाः स्मृताः ॥

Source: The standard enumeration verse for the eight sāttvika bhāvas, carried through the Nāṭyaśāstra and its later alaṃkāra-śāstra restatements (compare Sāhityadarpaṇa 3.128 and surrounding verses) — check exact chapter/verse against Ghosh's Nāṭyaśāstra translation before scholarly citation.

Translation: "Paralysis, then perspiration, then horripilation, voice-breaking, then trembling, discoloration, tears, and fainting — these eight are remembered as the sāttvika bhāvas."

Why it matters here: Every one of these eight is a documented, ordinary human physiological response to strong emotion — none is a stylized, arbitrarily-assigned code the way a hasta's specific finger-configuration is. This inverts RQ2/RQ6's finding for hasta: where hasta cleared only the conventionality bar and failed manifestation, sāttvika bhāvas plausibly clear a resemblance/manifestation-type bar and raise the opposite question about conventionality.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: sāttvika bhāvas cannot be tested with the identical instrument used for hasta, because the two channels invert each other's relationship to convention and resemblance. 13.3 Does Natural Resemblance Help or Hurt Sāttvika's Claim to Semiotic Status?

The honest answer cuts against the naive intuition that "more natural" means "more expressive" or "more linguistic." A signal that directly resembles what it signifies — tears resembling actual grief-tears — is, on the tradition's own lokadharmī/nāṭyadharmī distinction (Ch. 33 above and §1.5 of the white paper), closer to lokadharmī (ordinary worldly behavior) than to nāṭyadharmī's stylized convention. And RQ6 (Ch. 33–36) established that conventional, not natural, coding is the necessary condition for linguistic status. By that same standard applied consistently, sāttvika bhāvas — precisely because they succeed at naturalistic resemblance — fail the conventionality bar that hasta, awkwardly, passed. The two channels are not comparable on a single scale from "less linguistic" to "more linguistic"; they occupy genuinely different positions relative to the same criterion, one passing where the other fails and vice versa.

Acoustic

Comparable to distinguishing a genuinely natural acoustic cue (a person's real cry of pain) from a stylized theatrical cue built to resemble it (a sound-effect cry) — the natural one is not thereby "more of a code," it is arguably less of one, precisely because it isn't arbitrary.

Neuroscience

Parallels documented findings that spontaneous, felt facial expressions (Duchenne smiles, genuine distress expressions) are processed via more automatic, less cortically-mediated pathways than deliberately produced, learned expressions — resemblance-based signals and convention-based signals recruit different systems, and "natural" does not mean "more communicative" in any simple sense.

Biosynthesis

Like distinguishing a constitutive physiological stress response (cortisol release under real threat) from an engineered signaling analogue built to mimic it for a specific downstream effect — the natural response is not "more of a code" for that reason; if anything it is the pre-semiotic baseline a deliberate code would have to be built to imitate.

13.4 Testing Productivity: Do Sāttvika Bhāvas Combine?

RQ4 (Ch. 19–24) found hasta shares a phoneme inventory's finite, digital shape but not its combinatorial productivity. Sāttvika bhāvas fail even the shape-parallel: the eight bhāvas are not typically taught as combining into larger compound units the way saṃyukta hastas combine two asaṃyuta shapes (RQ9, Ch. site-extras-rq9 above). Documented performance practice does describe co-occurring bhāvas (trembling accompanied by tears, pallor accompanied by voice-breaking) but this is closer to RQ9's finding for symmetric-bilateral saṃyukta hastas: simultaneous co-occurrence without any documented combinatorial rule generating new, third meanings from the pairing. Two bhāvas occurring together intensify a single emotional reading; they do not compose into a distinct further bhāva the way (in RQ9's asymmetric case) two named hastas compose into Śivaliṅga's distinct third referent.

13.5 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 13 Closed

Sāttvika bhāvas, tested against the same evidentiary bars applied to hasta throughout RQ1–RQ9, occupy a genuinely different and in some respects inverse position: naturalistic rather than conventionally coded, non-combinatorial in a stronger sense than hasta (no documented compounding rule at all, not even the closed-list compounding RQ9 found), and functioning primarily as a direct physiological contribution to rasa rather than as a discrete, nameable sign carrying denotable content. This is not a weaker finding than RQ1–RQ9's hasta-focused results — it is a structurally different one, and stating it plainly prevents readers from assuming "sāttvika" simply behaves like a lower-fidelity version of āṅgika. The two channels are doing different kinds of semiotic work within the same fourfold coordinated system.

New Cluster · RQ 14Tantric and Yogic Mudrā: A Structurally Different System Worth Distinguishing

Every prior cluster tests hasta as found in the Nāṭyaśāstra-descended dramaturgical tradition — a system built for audience-facing communication within performance. A second, historically distinct family of hand-configurations, also called mudrā, appears in Tantric ritual and haṭha-yogic practice. This cluster asks a scoping question the document has not yet made explicit: are these the same kind of system as dramaturgical hasta, such that findings about one transfer to the other, or are they different enough in function that treating them together would blur two genuinely separate semiotic phenomena?

14.1 Two Traditions, One Overlapping Vocabulary

Some mudrā names and even hand-shapes overlap between the dramaturgical and tantric-yogic traditions — Añjali (the salutation hasta tested in the saṃyukta case studies above) appears in devotional and yogic contexts as well as on stage. But the overlap in name and shape should not be read as overlap in function. A dramaturgical hasta is constitutively addressed to a perceiving audience (Ch. 1's shloka box: perceptibility-for-another is vaikharī's defining property, and RQ1 extends exactly that property to gesture). A tantric mudrā, by contrast, is frequently performed with no audience present at all, as part of a practitioner's own ritual or meditative practice — its documented function is to produce or channel an internal psychophysical or ritual effect for the practitioner, not to communicate a graspable content to a second party.

14.2 Testing Tantric Mudrā Against RQ1's Own Defining Property

This is a direct, clean application of RQ1's own test (Ch. 1–8): does the practice in question have vaikharī's defining property, perceptibility-for-another? For dramaturgical hasta, performed before an audience whose reception is the entire point, the answer is yes, which is exactly why Ch. 1–8's extension argument was built around that property in the first place. For a solitary tantric practitioner's mudrā, performed with no perceiving other present and no documented claim that its efficacy depends on being seen or understood by anyone, the answer is no — at least not in the sense RQ1 tested. This places solitary tantric mudrā practice in a position closely related to RQ10's paśyantī-boundary case (§10 above): both are instances where a hand-configuration exists, and is even named and taught, but the vaikharī-extension's own defining property is absent.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

मुद्रा देवस्य संतोषकारिणी दुःखनाशिनी । स्वयमेव फलं दत्ते मुद्रितं कर्म यत्नतः ॥

mudrā devasya santoṣakāriṇī duḥkhanāśinī | svayam eva phalaṃ datte mudritaṃ karma yatnataḥ ||

Source: A representative verse in the idiom of the haṭha-yoga and tantric mudrā-nirūpaṇa manuals (compare the mudrā chapters of the Haṭhayogapradīpikā and Gheraṇḍasaṃhitā) — treat this as a paraphrase-verse representative of the genre rather than a page-verified citation from a specific edition; check against Svātmārāma's or Gheraṇḍa's critical editions before scholarly citation.

Translation: "The mudrā, pleasing to the deity, destroys suffering; the action sealed by it yields its fruit of its own accord, through the practitioner's own effort."

Why it matters here: Note what this verse does not claim: it makes no reference to a perceiving audience, a communicated meaning, or a second party grasping anything at all. The mudrā's stated effect is direct — it "yields its fruit of its own accord" through the practitioner's own sealed action. This is about as far as a hand-configuration can get from vaikharī's defining property while still being called, in Sanskrit, a mudrā — which is precisely why treating tantric and dramaturgical mudrā as a single undifferentiated category would be a category error this document is careful to avoid.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: RQ1–RQ13's entire evidentiary apparatus was built for, and should be scoped to, the dramaturgical hasta tradition specifically. Tantric and yogic mudrā are a related but structurally distinct phenomenon, best analyzed on their own terms (ritual efficacy, practitioner-internal psychophysical effect) rather than folded into a semiotic framework built for audience-facing communication.

14.3 A Documented Exception: Mudrā in Devotional Performance Contexts

The scoping claim above needs one honest qualification. In devotional performance traditions — temple dance, bhajan-accompanying gesture, and ritual dance-drama performed before a congregation or a deity-image treated as a perceiving witness — tantric-style mudrā are sometimes performed in a context that does restore something like vaikharī's defining property, because the deity-image is ritually treated as a perceiver, and the human congregation is an actual perceiving audience even where the deity's perception is a matter of faith rather than empirical fact. This is a documented middle case: neither purely solitary tantric practice nor purely secular dramaturgical performance, but a hybrid that the document's binary framing (dramaturgical vs. tantric) does not cleanly capture.

Acoustic

Comparable to the difference between a transmitted radio signal (built for a receiver) and an unbroadcast internal test-tone generated purely to calibrate an instrument — same underlying waveform-generating mechanism, entirely different functional status depending on whether a receiver is part of the design.

Neuroscience

Parallels the documented distinction between communicative and non-communicative motor acts recruiting different networks even when the movements look identical — a wave to a friend and an anatomically identical private stretching motion are processed differently because intended audience, not motor form, is what the brain treats as decisive.

Biosynthesis

Like a hormone that acts locally within the cell that produces it (autocrine signaling) versus the same molecule released to act on a distant target (endocrine signaling) — identical chemistry, entirely different classification depending on whether there is a genuine "receiver" downstream.

14.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 14 Closed

Dramaturgical hasta and tantric-yogic mudrā share vocabulary and sometimes shape, but differ on the exact property (perceptibility-for-another) this entire document's extension argument rests on. Solitary tantric practice lacks that property and should not be analyzed with the same instrument; devotional performance contexts occupy a documented middle position and deserve their own separate treatment rather than being silently absorbed into either category. This is a scoping clarification, not a new empirical finding about gesture's linguistic status — but scoping clarifications of this kind are exactly what the Method note (opening the white paper) commits this whole project to making explicit rather than leaving assumed.

New Cluster · RQ 10The Untested Fourth Channel: Sāttvika Bhāva

Ch. 26 (mūla śloka) named all four abhinaya channels — āṅgika, vācika, āhārya, sāttvika — at the point of definition, and Ch. 27–29 tested coordination between āṅgika and vācika in detail. But sāttvika itself was never separately tested against the same evidentiary bar RQ2 and RQ6 applied to āṅgika. That is a real gap, because sāttvika is not a minor channel: Abhinavagupta's own commentary treats it as the channel closest to rasa itself. This cluster closes the gap by running the identical tests — conventionality, sign-type, dhvani-fit — against a channel that is, on its face, the opposite of āṅgika in one crucial respect: it is involuntary, not trained.

10.1 What Sāttvika Claims, Restated at the Same Precision as Ch. 1

The Nāṭyaśāstra defines sāttvika bhāva as arising from sattva — a state of undistracted mental absorption — and manifesting as involuntary psychophysical states that overtake the performer when genuinely absorbed in the emotion being portrayed, rather than states the performer deliberately assembles gesture by gesture. This is a structurally different kind of claim than the aupacārika prayoga argument tested in Ch. 1–8. That argument extended vaikharī's externalization-for-another property to a trained, codified sign system. Sāttvika bhāva, on the tradition's own account, is not trained in the same sense at all — a performer is taught to cultivate the conditions under which it arises (through sustained absorption, or abhyāsa), but the specific bodily events themselves — tears, gooseflesh, a breaking voice — are described as arising involuntarily once that absorption is genuine, not as individually rehearsed and deployed the way a hasta is.

Acoustic

The difference between stambha and a hasta is the difference between a genuine vocal-fold tremor under real emotional load and a deliberately produced vibrato — both audible, only one involuntary. Acoustic-phonetics research on emotional speech already treats these as separate categories (voice quality as an involuntary index vs. prosody as a controlled signal).

Neuroscience

Involuntary affect-expressive behavior recruits limbic and autonomic pathways (amygdala, hypothalamic-autonomic circuits) documented as largely distinct from the voluntary motor-planning pathways (premotor and supplementary motor cortex) that produce trained, codified gesture — a genuine mechanistic split, not just a descriptive one.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to distinguishing a constitutive stress-response cascade (triggered automatically past a threshold, like a heat-shock response) from an engineered, deliberately expressed synthetic pathway — both are real biological signals, produced by fundamentally different control logic.

10.2 Testing Sāttvika Against Ch. 12's Conventionality Criterion — And Getting the Opposite Answer

Ch. 12 found that āṅgika fails sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship and instead satisfies saṅketa (convention): a hasta's meaning must be explicitly taught, name by name. Sāttvika bhāva, tested on the same criterion, gives the opposite result for the paradigm case. Tears arising from genuine grief are not a taught, arbitrary pairing between a shape and a meaning — they are naturally, universally, cross-culturally associated with grief, in a way that does not require saṅketa to be intelligible even to an untrained observer. This is not a minor variation on āṅgika's finding; it is a structurally different position on the same test.

This has an immediate and important consequence for the sphoṭa-versus-dhvani argument built across Ch. 9–16 and Ch. 37–39. That argument was built and is valid specifically for āṅgika. It was never claimed, and should not be assumed, to extend automatically to sāttvika — a channel whose paradigm cases are naturally rather than conventionally linked to their meaning is, if anything, closer to sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship than āṅgika ever was, precisely on the feature (c) test that was decisive against āṅgika in Ch. 12.

10.3 The Complication: Staged Sāttvika and the Lokadharmī/Nāṭyadharmī Line

This finding needs one immediate qualification, because performance practice complicates the "naturally manifested" reading. A trained performer does not, in most staged contexts, wait for genuine autonomic grief before producing stage-tears; skilled performers are documented as able to produce a controlled approximation of sāttvika states — a controlled vaivarṇya, a summoned tremor — through technique. This is precisely the lokadharmī/nāṭyadharmī distinction Ch. 33 already established: sāttvika bhāva has a naturalistic register (lokadharmī — the real thing, arising involuntarily) and a stylized, technically produced register (nāṭyadharmī — a trained simulation of the real thing for staging purposes).

Once staged, technically-produced sāttvika is, in an important sense, re-conventionalized — a trained performer's controlled tremor is closer to a codified sign than to the spontaneous original it represents. The honest finding is therefore register-dependent rather than a single flat verdict: genuine, lokadharmī sāttvika bhāva is naturally linked to its meaning and sits closer to sphoṭa's manifestation relationship; staged, nāṭyadharmī sāttvika bhāva is a trained approximation and sits closer to āṅgika's conventionalized profile. Both registers are legitimate and both are named in the tradition's own vocabulary — this cluster's contribution is making explicit which register each specific claim about sāttvika bhāva applies to.

Acoustic

Directly parallel to the documented distinction in voice-quality research between genuine emotional vocal perturbation and an actor's trained simulation of it — acoustic analysis can sometimes, though not always, distinguish the two, exactly the register split this section names.

Neuroscience

Maps onto documented findings that trained affect-simulation (actors producing convincing facial expressions on cue) recruits voluntary motor-cortical pathways alongside, and sometimes partially substituting for, the involuntary limbic pathways engaged during genuine affect.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to an engineered cell line producing a molecule that is structurally identical to one made by a natural constitutive pathway — same output, different control mechanism, and the difference matters for how the system should be modeled.

10.4 Sāttvika as Index Rather Than Symbol — A Peircean Sharpening

The Cross-Tradition Comparison table above (Peirce's icon/index/symbol trichotomy) already noted that this tradition discusses resemblance, convention, and manifestation without formally trichotomizing them the way Peirce does. Sāttvika bhāva is exactly the case where that formal Peircean vocabulary earns its keep. Genuine, lokadharmī sāttvika bhāva is best classified as an index — a sign causally produced by the state it signifies, the way smoke indexes fire — rather than as a symbol in Peirce's sense, which is a sign conventionally, arbitrarily paired with its meaning (the category āṅgika was shown to belong to in Ch. 12). Staged, nāṭyadharmī sāttvika bhāva, by contrast, drifts toward symbolic status precisely because it is trained and reproduced on cue rather than causally triggered.

This sharpens rather than undermines the earlier chapters' findings: it confirms that the tradition's own materials already contain, in nascent form, exactly the distinction Peirce later formalized — a genuine convergence worth noting for the same reason Ch. 31's sign-language benchmark and Ch. 37's dhvani argument were both worth noting, as independent external confirmation of a distinction this tradition draws in its own terms.

10.5 Does Sāttvika Fit Dhvani, or Does It Need Its Own Model?

Ch. 37–39 proposed dhvani as the better-fitting model for āṅgika specifically because sphoṭa's manifestation-relationship failed for āṅgika's general case. Sāttvika bhāva presents close to the reverse situation: on 10.2's finding, genuine sāttvika bhāva is closer to a manifestation-relationship than āṅgika ever was. This means the dhvani-over-sphoṭa argument should not be extended to genuine, lokadharmī sāttvika bhāva without separate justification — doing so would repeat exactly the error Ch. 4 warned against: assuming a property established for one case transfers automatically to a structurally different one.

That said, dhvani retains real purchase even here, for a narrower reason. Rasa theory's own technical vocabulary treats sāttvika bhāva as one of the strongest vyañjakas (suggestors) of rasa — arguably the strongest of the four abhinaya channels for this specific purpose, because an audience's qualified perception (sahṛdaya) of a performer's genuine or convincingly staged tremor is precisely what dhvani theory calls suggestion rather than direct statement: the tremor does not denote grief the way a word does, it evokes grief in a prepared perceiver. So the finding is layered rather than contradictory: sāttvika's sign-meaning relationship (indexical/natural) is not well modeled by dhvani, but sāttvika's function within a rasa-producing performance (evoking, not stating) is a dhvani-style operation. These are two different questions, and this cluster's contribution is separating them explicitly rather than letting one answer stand in for both.

New Cluster · RQ 11Cross-Cultural Benchmark: Testing Universality Against Independent Gestural Systems

Ch. 31 used natural sign-language linguistics as an external check on one specific claim: that channel, not documented structure, is what determines linguistic status. This cluster extends the same benchmarking discipline to a different question the earlier chapters left open — whether the specific structural findings reached for the hasta system (digital categorization, low productivity, concatenative rather than generative combination, register-dependent naturalism) are a general property of codified performance-gesture systems, or a peculiar feature of this one textual tradition. Three independently developed, textually unrelated systems are tested against the same criteria: Japanese Noh kata, Balinese/Javanese mudra-and-mask vocabularies, and the 19th-century Western Delsarte system together with Laban Movement Analysis.

11.1 Japanese Noh Kata: A Independently Developed Codified-Gesture Tradition

Noh theatre's kata — a fixed vocabulary of named, stylized movement patterns — developed from the 14th century onward under Zeami and his successors, with no documented textual contact with the Sanskrit dramaturgical tradition. If the structural findings reached above for the hasta system are artifacts of this one textual lineage, Noh kata would be expected to look structurally different. If instead they reflect a more general property of how codified performance-gesture systems tend to organize themselves, Noh kata should show a comparable profile.

The comparison holds up on several of the tested dimensions. Kata are individually named, taught through direct imitation of a master (a closed, non-generative pedagogical method closely resembling how hastas are documented as taught), and combined within a performance according to convention and narrative context rather than a generative combinatorial grammar — the same concatenative-not-generative profile Ch. 20–21 documented for hasta sequences. Noh's own aesthetic vocabulary distinguishes jo-ha-kyū (a structuring principle of gradual intensification) as governing how kata are sequenced across a performance, which functions similarly to how nāṭyadharmī convention governs hasta sequencing — a convention-governed ordering principle, not a syntax in the linguistic sense.

11.2 Balinese and Javanese Mudra-and-Mask Systems

Balinese topeng (masked dance-drama) and Javanese wayang wong combine a codified hand-gesture vocabulary with masks that themselves carry fixed conventional meaning — structurally close to the āhārya-āṅgika coordination already documented for the Sanskrit tradition (10.2's vaivarṇya case study above is a direct parallel: a bodily sign depending on a costume/staging element for full legibility). Balinese performance vocabulary, like the Sanskrit hasta system, is documented as regionally variable across villages and lineages even within Bali itself — directly paralleling RQ5's finding (§5) that regional and textual divergence is the expected profile of a living, orally-transmitted codified sign system rather than evidence against the system's coherence.

One genuine point of difference is worth reporting honestly rather than smoothing over: some documented Balinese mask-gesture combinations are read by trained audiences as carrying more context-independent, stable meaning than the Sanskrit hasta system's own polysemous items (like Pātāka, case-studied above) — suggesting that the degree of context-dependent polysemy dhvani theory is built to accommodate (Ch. 38) may not be a universal feature of all codified gestural systems, but rather a specific characteristic of how the Sanskrit tradition's inventory happens to be structured. This is a genuine limitation of the dhvani-over-sphoṭa proposal's generalizability beyond the source tradition, and it is reported here rather than left implicit.

11.3 Delsarte and Laban: A Western Attempt at the Same Project

François Delsarte's 19th-century system for codifying expressive gesture, and Rudolf Laban's 20th-century Effort/Shape framework for movement analysis, represent an independently developed Western attempt to systematize exactly the phenomenon this document has been testing in the Sanskrit tradition — codified, meaningful, trainable gesture. Neither system engaged the Sanskrit material directly. The comparison is instructive less for structural agreement than for a difference in ambition worth naming precisely: Delsarte's system aimed at a claimed universal, near-natural correspondence between specific body zones and specific emotional categories — a stronger manifestational claim than this document found the Sanskrit hasta system could sustain (Ch. 12's decisive negative finding).

Laban's Effort/Shape framework, by contrast, deliberately avoids fixed sign-meaning pairings altogether, instead describing movement along continuous qualitative dimensions (weight, time, space, flow) — structurally closer to sāttvika bhāva's graded, non-digital profile (10.2 above) than to āṅgika's digital, categorically-taught hasta inventory. The three-way comparison (Sanskrit hasta / Delsarte / Laban) therefore maps onto a real spectrum from strongly digital-conventional to strongly continuous-natural, with the Sanskrit tradition's own two channels — āṅgika and sāttvika — occupying two different points on that same spectrum, a finding RQ10 already established from within the tradition and RQ11 now confirms from outside it.

11.4 What Four Independent Systems Converging Actually Establishes

Four textually unrelated traditions — Sanskrit, Japanese, Balinese/Javanese, and 20th-century Western movement analysis — converge on the same structural split this document derived from the Sanskrit sources alone: codified performance-gesture systems tend toward digital, non-generative, convention-governed organization, while involuntary or deliberately non-coded expressive movement tends toward continuous, graded organization. This convergence is genuine evidence for a general claim, but it should be stated at the same precision the rest of this document insists on: it supports the claim that this structural split is a common feature of how humans organize codified performance-gesture systems, not the stronger claim that the Sanskrit tradition's specific findings (the exact degree of polysemy, the specific dhvani-based model) generalize word-for-word to every other tradition. §11.2's honest report of Balinese mask-gesture's lower polysemy is the concrete reminder that the general structural pattern and the specific theoretical model built to explain one tradition's version of it are two different levels of claim.

Acoustic

Four independently evolved languages showing the same broad phonological universals (a small closed consonant-vowel inventory, hierarchical syllable structure) is treated in linguistics as strong evidence for a general cognitive constraint — without implying any two languages share identical phoneme inventories or phonotactic rules. The gestural parallel here is exactly this pattern.

Neuroscience

Parallels cross-cultural facial-expression research (building on Ekman's cross-cultural studies) finding a shared core of recognizable affect displays alongside genuine, documented cultural "display rules" governing when and how intensely they are shown — a shared substrate with real, non-trivial cultural variation layered on top.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to convergent evolution: unrelated lineages (birds and bats evolving flight independently) arriving at structurally similar solutions under similar functional pressures — the similarity is real and informative precisely because it arose without shared ancestry, not despite that fact.

New Cluster · RQ 12The Rasasūtra: Where Āṅgika and Sāttvika Both Terminate

Every earlier cluster has tested a channel or a mechanism in isolation — āṅgika's sign-type (RQ2, RQ6), its combinatorics (RQ4, RQ9), sāttvika's sign-type (RQ10), and cross-tradition structure (RQ11). None of them has yet asked the question the tradition itself treats as primary: what is all of this coordination for. The Nāṭyaśāstra's own answer is the rasasūtra — the single most debated verse in the entire Sanskrit dramaturgical tradition. This cluster closes the document's arc by showing how RQ1–RQ11's separate findings about āṅgika and sāttvika are, on Abhinavagupta's own reading, two coordinated routes to the same destination.

12.1 The Verse Itself, and Why It Generated Four Centuries of Rival Theories

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse — The Rasasūtra

विभावानुभावव्यभिचारिसंयोगाद्रसनिष्पत्तिः

vibhāvānubhāvavyabhicārisaṃyogād rasaniṣpattiḥ

Source: Nāṭyaśāstra 6.31 (numbering varies slightly by edition) — the single most extensively commented-on sūtra in the entire tradition, cited and re-glossed by nearly every major alaṃkāra-śāstra author from Bhaṭṭa Lollaṭa onward. Verify against the GOS or Kāvyamālā edition before citing the exact kārikā number.

Translation: Rasa is accomplished (niṣpatti) through the coming-together (saṃyoga) of the determinants (vibhāva), the consequents (anubhāva), and the transitory states (vyabhicāribhāva).

Prakriyā (how it works here): Note precisely which term is missing from this list: sāttvikabhāva is not named in the sūtra itself, even though Bharata treats it as one of the four constitutive abhinaya channels (Ch. 26) and devotes a full enumeration to it (RQ10's mūla śloka). This omission is exactly what generated centuries of commentarial dispute — is sāttvika bhāva folded into anubhāva (the visible consequents), or does it operate differently? RQ10's finding that sāttvika bhāva is structurally distinct from āṅgika (involuntary vs. trained, indexical vs. conventional) turns out to matter directly here: Abhinavagupta's own resolution, discussed in 12.3 below, depends on treating sāttvika bhāva as a privileged, especially reliable subtype of anubhāva — because its involuntary, non-conventional character (established independently in RQ10) makes it a more trustworthy sign of genuine inner state than a codified, conventionally-produced anubhāva like a hasta could ever be.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: the rasasūtra is where RQ2's sphoṭa-rejection, RQ6's linguistic-status finding, RQ10's indexical-sāttvika finding, and Ch. 37–39's dhvani proposal all have to answer to the same higher-order question at once — not whether gesture means something, but whether it can produce rasa, which the tradition treats as a stronger and different achievement than mere denotation.

12.3 Why Sāttvika Bhāva Is, on This Reading, the More Trustworthy Channel

Abhinavagupta's commentary on the rasasūtra gives sāttvika bhāva a privileged evidentiary status precisely because of the property RQ10 established independently: its involuntary, non-conventional production. A hasta can be produced by a performer with no corresponding inner state at all — nothing about producing Pātāka requires the performer to feel anything. A genuine tremor (vepathu) or genuine tears (aśru) are, by RQ10's own finding, much harder to produce without some corresponding absorption, which is exactly why Abhinavagupta treats sāttvika bhāva as the anubhāva category spectators find most convincing — not because it is more meaningful in the semantic sense RQ2–RQ9 tested, but because its causal, indexical relationship to inner state (established independently via Peirce in 10.4) makes it harder to fake and therefore more evidentially weighty within the rasa-production process specifically.

This produces a genuinely interesting asymmetry worth stating precisely: āṅgika is the more linguistically sophisticated channel by every measure RQ2–RQ9 tested (closed inventory, digital categorization, some combinatorial structure, conventionally taught), while sāttvika is the evidentially more trustworthy channel specifically for rasa-production, for the opposite reason — because it resists exactly the kind of deliberate, trainable manipulation that gives āṅgika its structure. The two channels are not competing for the same job; they do different, complementary work within the fourfold coordination Ch. 26 established, and RQ10–RQ12 together explain why the tradition needed both rather than treating one as a redundant elaboration of the other.

Acoustic

Parallels the documented distinction in forensic voice analysis between a controllable acoustic cue (word choice, prosodic emphasis) and a harder-to-fake one (micro-tremor under genuine stress) — the harder-to-fake signal is weighted more heavily as evidence precisely because it resists deliberate manipulation.

Neuroscience

Directly parallel to documented findings in deception research: involuntary autonomic leakage (micro-expressions, genuine pupil dilation) is treated as more diagnostic of true internal state than voluntarily controlled expressive behavior, for the identical reason Abhinavagupta gives sāttvika bhāva evidentiary priority.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to how a constitutive biomarker (one the organism cannot suppress on demand) is treated as more diagnostically reliable in a clinical assay than an inducible one that could in principle be triggered artificially — reliability tracks resistance to manipulation, not complexity of signal.

12.4 Closing the Loop: How RQ1 and RQ12 Meet

Ch. 1–8 established that abhinaya is licensed as vaikharī's further reach on one specific, narrow property: externalization-for-another. RQ12 closes the document's arc by showing what that externalization is ultimately in service of, on Abhinavagupta's own account: not communication in the narrow linguistic sense RQ2–RQ9 spent thirty-two chapters carefully bounding, but the manifestation of rasa in a prepared perceiver — a communicative act, yes, but one whose success condition is aesthetic relish (bhukti/āsvāda) rather than propositional understanding. This is precisely why the document's central finding — that gesture is dhvani-style suggestion rather than sphoṭa-style or fully linguistic denotation — was the theoretically appropriate model all along: dhvani theory and rasa theory are, in Abhinavagupta's own synthesis in the Locana, the same theoretical apparatus applied to poetry and to drama respectively. RQ12 is not a new argument bolted onto the first eleven; it is where they were always heading.

13.1 What Āhārya Claims, and Why It Looked Dispensable

Āhārya covers everything a performer wears or carries that is not the body itself: costume (veṣa), makeup (aṅgaracanā), ornament (alaṃkāra), and hand-held or staged properties. Unlike āṅgika (trained sign), sāttvika (involuntary sign), and vācika (spoken/sung channel), āhārya produces no signal of its own — it has no independent semantic content the way a hasta or a word does. This is exactly why it was easy to treat as decorative support rather than a fourth channel doing real communicative work, the same dismissive move Ch. 22 of the white paper warns against for gesture generally.

13.2 Testing Āhārya's Actual Function: Enabling Condition, Not Fourth Sign-System

Vaivarṇya's case study (§9, sixth case) already showed one concrete instance: a sāttvika bhāva's visibility to an audience depending on lighting and staging support. Generalizing that single case, āhārya's documented role across the tradition is threefold: it fixes character identity before a word or gesture is produced (a crown identifies a king on sight); it extends āṅgika's own vocabulary by supplying props that combine with hastas concatenatively (Ch. 20–21's already-established pattern — recall Kaṭakāmukha's dependence on mimed objects); and it makes sāttvika bhāva legible at performance distance where an unaided face might not read. In none of these three roles does āhārya itself carry an independent, conventionally-taught meaning the way a hasta does — a specific crown does not mean "kingship" the way Pātāka means "flag" through learned saṅketa; it identifies a role through direct, practical association, closer to a uniform than to a lexical item.

13.3 The One Exception: Āhārya as Genuinely Conventional Sign

One documented category complicates the clean "enabling condition, not sign" reading and deserves honest treatment rather than being smoothed over: certain āhārya elements are conventionally coded in exactly the saṅketa sense Ch. 12 established for āṅgika. Color-coded makeup in Kathakali (the pacca/green for noble characters, kathi/knife-edged red-and-black for villainous ones, tāṭi for demonic types) is taught, name by name, the same way a hasta is — an audience member unfamiliar with the convention cannot read a character's moral type from the makeup any more than an untrained observer can read Pātāka's meaning from its shape. This is a genuine counter-case to 13.2's general finding, and it should be named as one rather than folded silently into the enabling-condition reading.

The honest resolution is that āhārya is not uniform across its own category. Costume identifying a specific named role (a crown for a king) functions as an enabling condition; makeup-color systems coding moral/psychological type (Kathakali's veṣa categories) function as a genuinely conventional sign system, closer in structure to āṅgika than to a chaperone-like support role. This is the same kind of internal split RQ10 found within sāttvika bhāva between its genuine and staged registers — a single named channel in the fourfold scheme turning out, on closer inspection, to contain more than one structural type.

14.2 Testing Netrābhinaya Against RQ10's Āṅgika/Sāttvika Split

RQ10 (§10.2) drew a clean structural line: āṅgika digital-conventional, sāttvika continuous-natural. Netrābhinaya sits directly on that line rather than cleanly on either side. The named dṛṣṭi categories are taught and drilled the way a hasta is — a performer learns sāci, prālokita, and the rest as discrete, nameable units, satisfying RQ2's saṅketa criterion (Ch. 12) in the same way āṅgika does. But the physiological substrate — actual eye-muscle movement — is continuous, and, crucially, involuntary gaze behavior (where the eyes go under real emotional load, documented in attention and emotion research as tracking genuine internal state) is a real phenomenon the trained dṛṣṭi system is explicitly built to approximate and control on stage, in the same lokadharmī/nāṭyadharmī sense RQ10 (§10.3) established for sāttvika bhāva generally.

This makes netrābhinaya the clearest single case in the entire document of a channel that has to be described with both halves of RQ10's typology simultaneously, rather than being assignable to one side. The correct description is not "netrābhinaya is āṅgika" or "netrābhinaya is sāttvika," but: a naturally continuous, sāttvika-adjacent physiological behavior has been given a digital, āṅgika-style codification and pedagogy, precisely because the underlying natural behavior (genuine gaze-shift under emotion) was worth training performers to approximate reliably on cue.

Acoustic

Comparable to how pitch, a continuous acoustic parameter, gets discretized into named tonal categories in tone languages — the underlying physical substrate stays continuous even after a digital, learnable category system is imposed on it for communicative reliability.

Neuroscience

Directly parallel to documented findings in eye-tracking and emotion research: genuine gaze-aversion and gaze-fixation patterns are read as reliable indices of underlying affective and attentional state, which is exactly the natural behavior the trained dṛṣṭi system is built to reproduce voluntarily.

Biosynthesis

Comparable to a regulatory system with both a constitutive baseline (always-on, continuously graded) and an inducible, trained overlay (switched on discretely under specific conditions) operating on the same underlying physiological substrate.

14.3 The Navarasa-Dṛṣṭi Convention: A Pedagogical, Not Canonical, System

Contemporary training in Bharatanatyam and Kathakali widely teaches a set of nine gaze-types keyed to the nine rasas (navarasa) — a distinct eye-configuration associated with each of śṛṅgāra, hāsya, karuṇa, raudra, vīra, bhayānaka, bībhatsa, adbhuta, and śānta. This convention should be reported with the same evidentiary caution this document applies elsewhere: it is a widely taught, pedagogically standard system in most living traditions, but it is not securely traceable to one single, precisely numbered canonical verse the way the eight sāttvika bhāvas are (RQ10's mūla śloka) — it functions more as an accumulated pedagogical synthesis, built up across teaching lineages from the Nāṭyaśāstra's more general dṛṣṭibheda material, than as a fixed textual unit itself.

This distinction matters for the same reason RQ5 (§5) mattered for the hasta inventory: treating a widely-taught living convention as if it had the same textual authority as a directly quotable mūla śloka would be exactly the false-precision this document has argued against from its opening method note. The navarasa-dṛṣṭi system is real, teachable, and consistent with the closed, digital profile §14.1 established — it is simply reported here at the correct, slightly lower evidentiary tier.

New Cluster · RQ 15Karaṇa as a Third Compositional Layer, Tested Against the Same Productivity Bar

RQ4 (Ch. 17–24) tested single-hand hasta against the phoneme inventory's productivity and found it lacking. RQ9 (site-extras-rq9) tested two-hand saṃyukta combination and found the same negative result at one level up. The Nāṭyaśāstra names a further, third compositional layer that neither prior cluster examined: karaṇa, the fixed combination of a sthāna (a named lower-body stance), a cārī (a named leg movement), and nṛtta-hasta (dance-hand positions, distinct from the abhinaya-hasta inventory RQ1–RQ9 tested) into a single, whole-body unit. This cluster asks whether karaṇa-level combination — now spanning the whole body rather than just the hands — is any more generative than the two prior layers proved to be.

15.1 What a Karaṇa Actually Combines

Bharata's Nāṭyaśāstra, Chapter 4, enumerates 108 karaṇas as the foundational units of tāṇḍava, the dance attributed to Śiva. Each karaṇa is documented as a fixed, named combination of three independently classifiable elements: a sthāna (such as samapāda, vaiśākha, or maṇḍala — named foot/weight-distribution positions), a cārī (a named trajectory of leg movement, of which the Nāṭyaśāstra separately enumerates thirty-two, sixteen bhaumī or ground-based and sixteen ākāśikī or aerial), and a hand position drawn from the nṛtta-hasta set (pure dance-hands, distinguished in the text from the abhinaya-hasta set this whole document has otherwise studied). A karaṇa is thus a compound object one level more complex than saṃyukta hasta: not two hands combining, but a stance, a leg-trajectory, and a hand-position combining across the whole body.

15.2 Testing Karaṇa Against Ch. 30's Design-Features Checklist

Ch. 30 (RQ3 cluster) established three general requirements for sentence-level generativity: productive combination, formal grammatical marking, and the capacity to generate genuinely novel propositions. Karaṇa passes the first requirement's surface form — three independently varying elements (sthāna, cārī, hand-position) do combine — but the same test RQ9.3 applied to saṃyukta hasta applies here with equal force: is the combination rule general and productive, capable of yielding karaṇas never individually listed, or is each of the 108 a separately named, separately taught, closed-list entry? The documented pedagogical practice is unambiguous on this point. Each of the 108 karaṇas is individually named (Talapuṣpapuṭa is standardly cited as the first in the sequence), individually described in its own verse, and individually taught — a student does not learn the general combination rule and then freely generate a 109th karaṇa from an unlisted sthāna-cārī-hasta triple. The mathematically possible combinations of the documented sthānas, cārīs, and nṛtta-hastas vastly exceed 108, which is itself evidence that the 108 are a curated, closed selection rather than the output of an applied general rule.

15.3 A Documented Complication: The Aṅgahāra Layer Suggests the Rule Might Operate One Level Up

Before closing this cluster too quickly, one complication deserves honest treatment. It is possible that karaṇa combination is closed-list precisely because the tradition's actual combinatorial productivity, if it exists at all, operates one level higher — at the aṅgahāra layer, where karaṇas themselves are combined into larger units. RQ17 below tests this directly, so RQ15's finding is stated provisionally here and revisited there.

Acoustic

Comparable to a fixed catalogue of named chord voicings in a musical tradition — each voicing is a real combination of independently varying notes, and the combination vocabulary (samāyoga) is genuine, but a closed, individually-taught catalogue of 108 named voicings is not the same claim as a fully generative harmonic grammar capable of producing voicings never catalogued.

Neuroscience

Parallels the distinction between a large but closed motor-vocabulary (a trained athlete's or dancer's repertoire of named, drilled compound movements) and a genuinely generative motor-planning system capable of producing entirely novel compound movements on the fly — both involve real combination of sub-components, but only the second meets a productivity criterion in Ch. 30's sense.

Biosynthesis

Like a fixed, evolutionarily selected set of protein folding conformations from a much larger space of chemically possible conformations — the folding process is a real combination of sub-structures, and the selected set is not arbitrary, but it is still a curated closed set rather than evidence of unbounded generative capacity.

15.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 15 Closed as Structurally Parallel to RQ9

Karaṇa combination, despite the tradition's own definitional vocabulary of samāyoga and despite operating across the whole body rather than just the hands, resolves into the same pattern RQ9 found for saṃyukta hasta: a real, textually intentional combination of independently classifiable elements, individually taught as a closed, named list of 108, rather than a productive rule generating unlisted compounds. The whole-body scale of the combination does not, by itself, confer the generativity that scale of combination alone cannot supply.

New Cluster · RQ 16The 108 Count Itself: Textual Enumeration vs. Epigraphic Divergence

RQ5 (site-extras §2) documented that the asaṃyuta hasta inventory diverges between the Nāṭyaśāstra's 24 and the Abhinaya Darpaṇa's 28, and argued this divergence refines rather than overturns the finding that the inventory is finite and closed. Karaṇa's 108-count invites the identical test, this time against a different kind of secondary source: not a later manuscript tradition, but stone.

16.1 The Textual Count and Its Epigraphic Witness

Bharata's Nāṭyaśāstra Chapter 4 names and enumerates 108 karaṇas in sequence, beginning with Talapuṣpapuṭa. The same 108-sequence is carved in low relief on the gopuram (temple gateway tower) of the Naṭarāja temple at Chidambaram, and a comparable, partially overlapping set appears at other temple sites including Kumbakonam and Vriddhachalam. These reliefs are not merely decorative echoes of the text; twentieth-century reconstructive scholarship (most notably the practice-based research of Padma Subrahmanyam, who used the Chidambaram reliefs alongside the textual verses to reconstruct karaṇas whose textual description alone was ambiguous) treats the stone carvings as an independent, physically fixed witness to what the numbered sequence actually looked like in practice, at the specific historical moment the reliefs were carved.

16.2 Where Text and Stone Agree, and Where They Diverge

The core sequence and numbering are stable between the Nāṭyaśāstra's textual enumeration and the Chidambaram reliefs — this is itself notable, given the centuries separating Bharata's text from the temple's construction, and it is the strongest available evidence that karaṇa was a genuinely stable, transmitted performance category rather than a purely literary invention. The divergence, where it exists, is of a different character than RQ5's hasta divergence: rather than additions or omissions to the list, the primary documented disagreement concerns the precise visual interpretation of individual karaṇas whose textual verse description is terse enough to admit more than one plausible reconstruction — exactly the interpretive gap Subrahmanyam's reconstructive project was built to address using the stone reliefs as a disambiguating check on the text.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

तलपुष्पपुटं नाम करणं प्रथमं स्मृतम् । ऊर्ध्वजानुसमारब्धं ...

talapuṣpapuṭaṃ nāma karaṇaṃ prathamaṃ smṛtam | ūrdhvajānu-samārabdham ...

Source: The opening verse describing Talapuṣpapuṭa, standardly cited as the first of the 108 karaṇas in the Nāṭyaśāstra's Chapter 4 sequence — this is given here as a representative opening fragment rather than a complete, independently re-verified quotation; the full verse and its exact reading should be checked against Ghosh's or Kavi's edition, and against the corresponding Chidambaram relief, before any scholarly citation.

Translation: "Talapuṣpapuṭa is the name remembered as the first karaṇa — begun with the knee raised..." (fragment; full description continues in the source verse).

Why it matters here: Talapuṣpapuṭa's position as the sequence's first-named entry, confirmed independently by its carved position at Chidambaram, is exactly the kind of cross-medium agreement RQ16 is testing for. Where a textual list's ordering is independently confirmed by an unrelated physical medium (stone relief, carved centuries after the text and maintained by an entirely separate institutional tradition — temple architecture and worship, not manuscript copying), the finite-and-stable-core claim RQ5 made for hasta is considerably strengthened when extended to karaṇa: this is not one manuscript tradition's say-so, but two independent transmission channels converging.

Phala (result for this cluster): Result: karaṇa's core sequence and numbering are stable across textual and epigraphic transmission, with disagreement concentrated at the level of visual reconstruction of ambiguous individual entries — a stronger and more specific confirmation of RQ5's general "stable core, variable margin" finding than the purely textual asaṃyuta hasta comparison alone could offer.

16.3 Why Stone Is a Different Kind of Evidence Than a Second Manuscript

It is worth being explicit about why this comparison is methodologically stronger than RQ5's Nāṭyaśāstra-versus-Abhinaya-Darpaṇa comparison, rather than merely a second instance of the same kind of test. A second manuscript tradition can, in principle, simply copy or elaborate on the first, so agreement between two texts is weaker evidence of independent stability than agreement between a text and an entirely different medium maintained by an entirely different institution (temple sculpture, commissioned and maintained by temple administration and royal patronage, not by the scribal-manuscript tradition that transmitted the Nāṭyaśāstra itself). Convergence across genuinely independent transmission channels is the stronger form of corroboration.

Acoustic

Comparable to confirming a reconstructed historical pronunciation not merely from a second written source (which could simply copy the first) but from an independent physical record — a preserved recording or a phonetic transcription made by an entirely separate observer using different instruments.

Neuroscience

Parallels the standard methodological preference in cognitive science for converging evidence from independent measurement techniques (behavioral data and neuroimaging data agreeing) over two behavioral studies alone, precisely because independent methods cannot share the same systematic bias.

Biosynthesis

Like confirming a proposed molecular structure through both spectroscopic data and independently solved crystallographic data, rather than through two spectroscopic runs alone — agreement across fundamentally different measurement modalities is the stronger form of confirmation.

16.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 16 Closed

The 108-karaṇa count is confirmed across two independent transmission channels — manuscript text and temple relief — with disagreement limited to the visual reconstruction of individual ambiguous entries rather than to the sequence's core content or count. This strengthens, with a stronger form of evidence than was available for RQ5's purely textual hasta comparison, the general finding that this tradition's codified movement-inventories have a stable, transmitted core even where individual details require careful reconstruction.

New Cluster · RQ 17Aṅgahāra as a Fourth Compositional Layer: Karaṇas Combined Into Larger Units

RQ15.3 flagged a complication and deferred it here: if karaṇa-level combination is closed-list rather than generative, does the tradition's actual combinatorial productivity — if it exists anywhere in this whole compositional hierarchy — operate one level higher, at the aṅgahāra, where the Nāṭyaśāstra documents karaṇas themselves being combined into larger units? This cluster tests that possibility directly, using the identical productivity bar applied at every prior layer.

17.1 What an Aṅgahāra Combines

The Nāṭyaśāstra documents thirty-two aṅgahāras, each a named sequence of several karaṇas (commonly cited as ranging from four to eight karaṇas per aṅgahāra, though the exact count varies by which aṅgahāra is examined) strung together into a single choreographic unit, itself further organized into recaka (preparatory) and completing phases. If karaṇa is the tradition's third compositional layer (hand-plus-stance-plus-leg-movement), aṅgahāra is unambiguously a fourth: karaṇas-plus-karaṇas, combined in sequence.

17.2 Testing the Fourth Layer Against the Same Bar

The test RQ9.3 and RQ15.2 both applied is precise and needs no modification here: does a general, productive rule combine karaṇas into aṅgahāras such that new, unlisted aṅgahāras could be generated from the combination rule alone, or is each of the thirty-two aṅgahāras itself a separately named, separately taught, closed-list sequence? The documented textual and pedagogical practice again answers the second way. Each aṅgahāra is individually named and its constituent karaṇa-sequence individually specified in its own verse; a performer does not learn a general "any four to eight karaṇas may be sequenced" rule and then freely generate a thirty-third, unlisted aṅgahāra. The aṅgahāra layer is, in this precise sense, structurally identical to the saṃyukta-hasta layer (RQ9) and the karaṇa layer (RQ15): a real, historically fixed compounding of the layer below it, taught as a closed list rather than derived from a live generative rule.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

करणानां तु या माला सोऽङ्गहार इति स्मृतः । त्रीणि चत्वारि पञ्च स्युः षडष्टौ वापि तत्र वै ॥

karaṇānāṃ tu yā mālā so 'ṅgahāra iti smṛtaḥ | trīṇi catvāri pañca syuḥ ṣaḍ aṣṭau vāpi tatra vai ||

17.3 The Pattern Across Four Layers, Stated Plainly

It is worth naming the full pattern now that four layers have been tested with the identical instrument. Hasta (RQ4): finite, digital, non-productive. Saṃyukta hasta (RQ9): a real second-order combination, still closed-list. Karaṇa (RQ15): a real third-order combination spanning the whole body, still closed-list. Aṅgahāra (RQ17): a real fourth-order combination of karaṇas, still closed-list, and the tradition's own "garland" metaphor names this directly. At no layer, from the single hand-shape to the thirty-two-strong aṅgahāra repertoire, does this document find a productivity mechanism meeting Ch. 30's design-features bar. This is not four independent negative findings; it is one finding, confirmed with increasing scale and increasing textual self-awareness (the garland metaphor is the clearest case of the tradition seemingly aware of its own compositional, non-generative character).

Acoustic

Comparable to a fixed suite of named musical compositions (rāgas' associated bandiśes or gats), each itself built from a smaller closed set of melodic phrases, in turn built from an even smaller closed set of svaras — real compositional depth at every level, and still, at the top of that hierarchy, a curated repertoire rather than an unbounded generative grammar.

Neuroscience

Parallels a highly drilled athletic or martial-arts curriculum where individual strikes combine into named combinations, combinations combine into named forms (kata), and forms combine into a named repertoire — real hierarchical composition at every level, processed and retrieved as chunked motor sequences rather than assembled from a productive generative rule each time.

Biosynthesis

Like a fixed metabolic pathway with several sequential, obligatory steps, each step itself a real combination of substrates — a genuine multi-level compositional structure that is nonetheless a specific, evolutionarily fixed pathway rather than evidence of a general, combinatorially unbounded synthetic capacity.

17.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 17 Closed

Aṅgahāra-formation, the fourth and highest compositional layer this document has tested, resolves into the same closed-list pattern found at every layer below it — now independently corroborated by the tradition's own "garland" metaphor for what an aṅgahāra actually is. RQ15.3's deferred question is answered: the productivity that fails at the karaṇa layer does not reappear at the aṅgahāra layer either. There is no layer, from single hasta through aṅgahāra, at which this tradition's movement-vocabulary meets a generative-grammar standard, and the consistency of that finding across four independently tested layers is itself the strongest evidence in the whole document for treating non-productivity as a genuine structural feature of the system, not an artifact of any one layer's limited data.

New Cluster · RQ 18The Devī-Saptaśatī Repertoire as a Performance Corpus: Rasa, Not Semiotic Source

A necessary scoping note before this cluster begins: the Devī Māhātmyam, commonly called Durgā Saptaśatī (seven hundred verses, across thirteen chapters and three caritas), is a devotional-narrative and mythological text. It does not discuss vaikharī, sphoṭa, dhvani, hasta, karaṇa, or aṅgahāra — it is not a semiotics or dramaturgy text of the kind this document otherwise draws on, and it would misrepresent the source to cite it as though it theorizes about gesture. What it legitimately provides is something different and still valuable to this document's project: a widely performed narrative corpus, staged in several regional dance-drama and nṛtya traditions using precisely the hasta/karaṇa/aṅgahāra vocabulary RQ1–RQ17 have tested, and dominated by a specific, narrow cluster of rasas. This cluster examines what the karaṇa/aṅgahāra system this document has now fully tested actually does when staging that specific narrative content.

18.1 The Rasa Profile of the Saptaśatī Narrative

The Saptaśatī's three caritas culminate, respectively, in the Devī's combat with Madhu and Kaiṭabha, with Mahiṣāsura, and with Śumbha and Niśumbha — battle narratives whose governing rasas, on the Nāṭyaśāstra's own eightfold (later ninefold) rasa scheme, are documented as predominantly raudra (fury), bhayānaka (terror, especially in the demonic armies' overwhelming approach), and vīra (heroism, in the Devī's own unwavering combat). This is a narrower and more combat-concentrated rasa profile than most of the classical dance repertoire's usual śṛṅgāra-dominant (romantic/erotic) material, and it is worth testing whether the karaṇa/aṅgahāra system — built, per RQ15–RQ17, from a fixed, closed, non-generative vocabulary — actually has the documented range to stage this different rasa profile convincingly, or whether it strains the system's own resources.

18.2 Karaṇa Names That Document Combat-Oriented Movement

Several of the 108 karaṇas carry names and descriptions directly suited to combat and vigorous, martial movement rather than the gentler lyrical vocabulary associated with śṛṅgāra-dominant repertoire — karaṇas involving vigorous leaps, turns, and forceful stance-changes are documented in the Nāṭyaśāstra's own descriptions and appear, in the epigraphic record RQ16 examined, alongside Naṭarāja's own tāṇḍava (the vigorous, cosmic dance form karaṇa is traditionally associated with in the first place, per the Talapuṣpapuṭa verse's own attribution). This is a case where the closed, non-generative repertoire RQ15–RQ17 documented is not thereby an impoverished one: a curated, finite set can still contain enough internal range — vigorous versus lyrical karaṇas, raudra-suited versus śṛṅgāra-suited hasta and sāttvika combinations — to stage a genuinely different rasa profile without needing generative productivity to do so.

18.3 Why This Confirms Rather Than Complicates the Non-Productivity Finding

It would be a mistake to read the Saptaśatī repertoire's successful staging of raudra/bhayānaka/vīra content as evidence against RQ15–RQ17's non-productivity finding — as though the system must be "generating" new combat-specific movement to meet this narrative's demands. The more precise reading, consistent with RQ9's dhvani-strengthening observation about closed-list compounds, is that a sufficiently large curated repertoire (108 karaṇas, 32 aṅgahāras, the full hasta and sāttvika inventories) has enough internal variety to be selectively deployed across a wide range of rasa registers without any single deployment requiring a new, unlisted unit to be generated. The Devī-Saptaśatī repertoire is best described as a documented selection from the existing closed vocabulary, weighted toward its more vigorous entries, not a demonstration of the vocabulary's generative extension.

New Cluster · RQ 19Piṇḍībandha as a Fifth Compositional Layer: From Solo to Group

RQ15–RQ17 tested three compositional layers — hasta, karaṇa, aṅgahāra — all built for and executed by a single performer. The Nāṭyaśāstra documents a further layer that changes the unit of composition itself: piṇḍībandha (also piṇḍī), a group formation in which multiple performers arrange their bodies into a single, named composite shape — a wheel, a lotus, a mountain, or a specific animal form — built from individually held karaṇas and sthānas distributed across several bodies at once. This cluster asks whether shifting from a single body's compositional hierarchy to a many-body one changes any prior finding about productivity.

19.1 What a Piṇḍībandha Actually Is

Where a karaṇa combines a stance, a leg-movement, and a hand-position within one performer's body, a piṇḍībandha combines several performers' individually held positions into a single collective shape recognizable from the audience's vantage point — the visual composite, not any one performer's individual movement, is the named unit. The Nāṭyaśāstra's own treatment of piṇḍī, srī, and similar group-formation terms places this squarely within the same dramaturgical (not tantric or purely decorative) tradition RQ1–RQ18 have tested throughout, typically deployed at moments of heightened spectacle — processions, divine assemblies, or, relevantly for RQ22 below, battle tableaux.

19.2 Testing Piṇḍībandha Against the Same Productivity Bar

The pattern established across RQ9, RQ15, and RQ17 repeats with striking consistency at this fifth layer. A piṇḍībandha's recognizable shape (a lotus, a wheel) is documented as a specific, named, historically fixed composition of individual body-positions — not a productive rule by which any arrangement of performers' bodies could be freely combined into an audience-recognizable new shape never previously named or taught. The choreographic knowledge required to form a "lotus" piṇḍī is passed down as a specific, rehearsed arrangement, exactly as a karaṇa or aṅgahāra is: a closed, curated composition, now distributed across multiple bodies rather than housed within one.

New Cluster · RQ 20The Mārgī/Deśī Distinction Applied Across the Whole Hierarchy

RQ5 documented textual divergence in the hasta inventory (Nāṭyaśāstra's 24 versus Abhinaya Darpaṇa's 28) and RQ16 documented textual-versus-epigraphic divergence in the karaṇa count. Both were treated as instances of a single phenomenon: variable margin, stable core. This cluster asks a related but distinct question using the tradition's own explicit vocabulary for exactly this kind of variation — the mārgī/deśī distinction — and checks whether that distinction, applied consistently, changes any conclusion reached so far.

20.1 What Mārgī and Deśī Actually Name

Mārgī denotes the "path" tradition — the pan-regional, textually canonized style the Nāṭyaśāstra itself claims universal, trans-regional validity for, associated with Bharata's own codification and, in later reception, with temple and royal-court performance contexts treated as authoritative. Deśī denotes "regional" or "local" practice — style, repertoire, and even named units documented as varying by region, and treated by later theorists (most systematically Śārṅgadeva in the Saṅgītaratnākara, discussed further in RQ21) as a legitimate, parallel category rather than a corruption of mārgī practice.

20.2 Testing Whether Mārgī/Deśī Maps Onto RQ5's and RQ16's Divergence Findings

It would be a natural but incorrect simplification to treat mārgī as simply "the stable core" and deśī as simply "the variable margin" RQ5 and RQ16 already documented. The textual record does not support so clean a mapping. Some of the Abhinaya Darpaṇa's additions to the Nāṭyaśāstra's asaṃyuta hasta list (RQ5.2) are themselves presented, within Nandikeśvara's own text, as mārgī-status refinements rather than deśī importations — meaning the mārgī category itself is not static across texts, but is periodically re-codified by later authorities claiming to state (not alter) universal practice. Deśī material, correspondingly, is not simply "everything mārgī excludes" — Śārṅgadeva's own deśī chapters document deśī tālas, deśī rāgas, and deśī movement-vocabulary as positively described systems in their own right, not as a residual category.

New Cluster · RQ 21Why Bharata's Own Text Is Not Enough: The Textual-Layering Problem Behind Karaṇa

RQ16 treated the Chidambaram reliefs as an independent witness confirming the Nāṭyaśāstra's karaṇa sequence. What that cluster did not examine is a well-known problem within Sanskrit dance scholarship itself: the Nāṭyaśāstra's own Chapter 4 verses describing individual karaṇas are, by wide scholarly agreement, frequently too terse and technically compressed to permit confident reconstruction from the text alone — which is exactly why the epigraphic and later commentarial record (including, centrally, Śārṅgadeva's thirteenth-century Saṅgītaratnākara) had to do real interpretive work rather than merely restating Bharata. This cluster examines that layering problem directly.

21.1 The Documented Terseness Problem

Nāṭyaśāstra Ch. 4's karaṇa verses typically name the karaṇa, then describe its constituent sthāna, cārī, and hand-position in extremely compressed technical vocabulary, assuming a reader already trained in the terminology of the surrounding chapters — a reasonable assumption for an embodied pedagogical tradition (where the text supplements a guru's direct physical demonstration) but a genuine obstacle for any purely textual reconstruction attempted centuries later once the living demonstration tradition for some regions had lapsed. This is precisely the gap Padma Subrahmanyam's Chidambaram-relief-based reconstruction (RQ16.1) was built to close, and it is precisely the gap Śārṅgadeva's Saṅgītaratnākara had already begun closing, in prose and elaborated verse, roughly seven centuries after Bharata.

21.2 What Śārṅgadeva's Saṅgītaratnākara Specifically Adds

The Saṅgītaratnākara does not merely repeat the Nāṭyaśāstra's karaṇa list — it is documented as providing fuller, more explicit descriptive detail for individual karaṇas and their constituent sthānas and cārīs than Bharata's own compressed verses supply, functioning as an intermediate interpretive layer between the terse root text and the fully reconstructed modern understanding. This makes Śārṅgadeva's text methodologically comparable to Abhinavagupta's Abhinavabhāratī commentary in the white paper's own Part I: a later, locatable, and technically substantive elaboration of an earlier terse root claim, rather than either a simple copy or an unrelated independent invention.

Mūla Śloka · Source Verse

भरतोक्तं करणजातं संक्षेपादिह वर्ण्यते । विस्तरेण तु तज्ज्ञेयं नाट्यशास्त्रानुसारतः ॥

21.3 Why This Strengthens Rather Than Weakens RQ16's Finding

It might seem that acknowledging Bharata's own terseness weakens the confidence RQ16 placed in the 108-count's stability. The opposite is closer to the truth. If the root text alone were fully sufficient and self-explanatory, later independent witnesses (Śārṅgadeva's elaboration, the Chidambaram reliefs, Subrahmanyam's twentieth-century reconstruction) converging on substantially the same sequence and count would be a comparatively weak form of confirmation — of course a clear text will be read the same way by everyone. That the root text is genuinely terse and technically demanding, and that independent elaborators and independent physical media nonetheless converge on the same core sequence, is the stronger form of evidence: convergence under difficulty, not convergence by default.

Acoustic

Comparable to independently reconstructing a degraded historical audio recording from multiple noisy copies — convergence on the same reconstructed signal from multiple imperfect sources is stronger evidence of the signal's authenticity than a single clean copy would provide on its own.

Neuroscience

Parallels how converging behavioral and neural evidence for a contested cognitive phenomenon, obtained by independent labs using imperfect and partially divergent methods, is treated as stronger confirmation than a single unreplicated finding, precisely because the difficulty of the underlying measurement makes accidental agreement unlikely.

Biosynthesis

Like confirming a difficult-to-resolve molecular structure through several independently imperfect experimental techniques that nonetheless converge on the same answer — the difficulty of each individual method is precisely what makes their agreement meaningful.

21.4 Cluster Synthesis: RQ 21 Closed

Bharata's own Nāṭyaśāstra Ch. 4 is documented as too compressed to fully self-explain the karaṇa system, which is exactly why Śārṅgadeva's Saṅgītaratnākara, centuries later, undertook real elaborative work rather than mere repetition — and exactly why the epigraphic record (RQ16) mattered as an independent check. Far from undermining RQ16's confidence in the 108-count's stability, this layering problem, honestly examined, is the reason that stability is well-evidenced: it survives transmission through a genuinely difficult, multi-layered textual and physical record, not through simple, low-friction copying.
